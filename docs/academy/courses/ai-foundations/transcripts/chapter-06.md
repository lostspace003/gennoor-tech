Chapter six. *Hands-on prompts that work.* Five patterns you can use today, in Copilot, ChatGPT, Claude, Gemini. *Tested. Practical. Boring. That's the point — these are the ones that work consistently.*

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 1]

Pattern one. *Role plus task plus format plus constraints plus examples.* The five-part recipe.

*Role* — "Act as an experienced HR business partner." *Task* — "Draft a response to this employee question." *Format* — "Three paragraphs, professional tone, action-oriented closing." *Constraints* — "No legal commitments. Refer ambiguous cases to legal." *Examples* — paste one or two reference responses.

This is the workhorse prompt. *Works across every model. Works across most use cases.* When in doubt, return to it.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 2]

Pattern two. *Ask for the structure first.* Before asking the model to write a draft, ask it to outline the structure. *"Outline the key sections of a board memo arguing for X."*

Why this works. *You catch problems before the model invests a thousand words in the wrong frame.* If the outline is wrong, you fix the frame. If the outline is right, *then* you ask for the draft.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 3]

Pattern three. *Verify the citation.* If the model gives you a specific fact, study, court case, or statistic — *ask it for the source. Then check the source actually exists.*

Mata v. Avianca is the cautionary tale from chapter four. *If the model produces a citation you can't verify, treat the underlying claim as unverified.* Don't ship it.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:45]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 4]

Pattern four. *Rewrite my draft for X audience.* You write the first draft. *The AI adapts it.* For your CEO. For your team. For a customer. For a regulator. *Each audience needs different vocabulary, different framing, different level of detail.*

This pattern works because the *substance is yours* — the substance the AI knows least about. The AI handles the adaptation — which it does well.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · pattern 5]

Pattern five. *Critique my draft.* You write something. You paste it. You ask the model — *"Find the three weakest arguments in this draft."* Or — *"Where would a hostile reviewer push back?"*

The AI is good at finding weaknesses you've stopped seeing. *You retain authorship. You gain a second pair of eyes.* This is the pattern most professionals underuse.

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:45]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · what to do this week]

The Monday move. *Pick one of the five patterns.* Use it this week, every time the relevant situation comes up. *Track how often. Track how useful.* If the pattern works, add a second one next week. *Two weeks. Two patterns. Habit forming.*

That's the foundation. The next chapter is *how to make it stick* — beyond the novelty phase.
