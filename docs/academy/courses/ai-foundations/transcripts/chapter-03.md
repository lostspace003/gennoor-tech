> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:55]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · five categories that work]

*Category one — drafting.* First drafts of emails, reports, summaries, proposals. *AI gets you eighty percent of the way to something usable in a fraction of the time.* You edit. The discipline is yours. The drafting is shared.

*Category two — summarising.* Long documents, meeting transcripts, research papers. *AI condenses faster than humans.* You verify the key facts.

*Category three — translating.* Between languages. Between registers. Between technical and non-technical audiences. *Modern LLMs are extraordinary at this.* Better than most humans on most pairs.

*Category four — classifying and extracting.* Pull specific data points from documents. Tag emails by intent. Categorise customer feedback. *Routine information work that used to take hours now takes minutes.*

*Category five — generating code.* If you write any code at all — *AI dramatically accelerates it.* GitHub Copilot, Cursor, Claude Code, Replit. Even non-programmers can now write basic scripts.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · what doesn't work as well]

What works less reliably in twenty twenty-six. *Three categories.*

*One — open-ended research with citations.* The hallucination problem from chapter two. *If you ask the AI to find specific cases or papers or studies, verify every single citation against the primary source.* Stanford's seventeen to thirty-four percent hallucination number is why.

*Two — judgemental decisions.* Hiring choices. Personnel decisions. Customer escalations. Anything where the answer depends on context only humans have, and where being wrong matters. *AI can inform. Humans decide.*

*Three — identity verification and specific facts about specific people.* The model wasn't trained on your customers. It doesn't know if a specific person did or said a specific thing. *Don't use it as a substitute for actually knowing.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the discipline]

The discipline. *Match the tool to the task.* AI for drafting, summarising, translating, classifying, code. Human judgement on the high-stakes stuff. *Verification of any specific fact before it leaves your desk.*

The Microsoft Work Trend Index — Microsoft's own published research on Copilot users — *reports around thirty percent time savings on routine documents for active users.* Real. But "active users" is the important phrase. *The benefit goes to people who use it consistently. Not people who tried it once.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pick one task in your week. One you do regularly. Drafting an update to your team. Summarising a long thread. Classifying customer feedback.* Use AI for that task for one full week. *Time it. Note quality differences. Decide whether to keep doing it.*
