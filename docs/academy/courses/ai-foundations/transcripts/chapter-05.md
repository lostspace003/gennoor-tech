Chapter five. *Evaluating AI claims.* In twenty twenty-six, every vendor pitches AI. Every product is "AI-powered." Every productivity number is impressive. *Three questions to ask before you believe any of it.*

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · Tetlock]

The framing. *Philip Tetlock — political-judgment researcher — twenty-plus years of work on forecasting performance.* His central finding — *calibration matters more than confidence.* A person who says "I'm seventy percent sure" and gets it right seventy percent of the time is more useful than someone who says "I'm certain" and gets it right eighty percent of the time. *The certain person is over-confident.*

Apply this to AI claims. *When a vendor says "thirty percent productivity gain," is that calibrated or over-confident?* That's what we're going to learn to ask.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · question 1]

Question one. *Studied population — who exactly?* Microsoft's Work Trend Index reports around thirty percent time savings on routine documents for *active Copilot users.* "Active" is the operative word. *People who use the tool consistently every week.* Not people who tried it once and shrugged.

If you're evaluating the claim — *ask which population.* Heavy users? Light users? People in marketing? People in engineering? *The claim that applies to your team may be different from the headline number.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · question 2]

Question two. *Compared to what?* "AI saved thirty percent of time on document drafting" — *compared to drafting without any assistance.* Not compared to drafting with Google. Not compared to using a template. Not compared to delegating to a junior colleague.

The honest comparison. *What was the workflow before? What is the workflow now? What's the time delta?* If the vendor can't answer that crisply, *the productivity number is marketing, not measurement.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · question 3]

Question three. *What didn't get measured?* Quality. Re-work. Verification time. The Stanford hallucination story is the cautionary tale — *the AI saved drafting time and added verification time.* If only drafting time is measured, the productivity story is incomplete.

Other things often unmeasured. *Re-work when AI output was wrong.* Cognitive offloading — does the team lose skills if AI does it for them. Bias and quality drift over time. *The honest vendor names what they didn't measure.* The marketing vendor doesn't.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · Pew]

One more useful number. *Pew Research twenty twenty-five — seventy percent of consumers feel more comfortable with AI agents when explicitly told they're AI.* Nine percent prefer hidden AI. *Disclosure improves trust.* If a vendor recommends hiding the AI nature of their tool, that's a signal worth paying attention to. *EU AI Act Article Fifty makes disclosure mandatory in the EU from August second twenty twenty-six anyway.*
