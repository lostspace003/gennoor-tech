Case one. *Mata versus Avianca — Southern District of New York — June twenty-second twenty twenty-three.* Two lawyers submitted a brief citing six federal court cases. *None of them existed.* All six were fabricated by ChatGPT.

The judge — Kevin Castel — sanctioned the lawyers *five thousand dollars and ordered them to write personal letters to each falsely-cited judge.* The lesson — *AI confidently produces specific facts that aren't real.* If you're going to use AI output in any high-stakes context, *verify every specific claim against a primary source.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:05]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · Air Canada]

Case two. *Moffatt versus Air Canada — British Columbia Civil Resolution Tribunal — February fourteenth twenty twenty-four.* Air Canada's website chatbot told a grieving passenger he could book first and apply for a bereavement-fare refund later. *Air Canada's actual policy required pre-approval.* The tribunal awarded the passenger eight hundred twelve Canadian dollars plus interest.

The line worth memorising — *"Air Canada is responsible for all information on its website, including from a chatbot."* The lesson — *anything an AI says on behalf of a company is the company's statement.* Doesn't matter that an AI generated it.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · Bloomberg]

Case three. *Bloomberg's resume study — March twenty twenty-four.* Bloomberg News investigators generated identical resumes, varied only by demographically-distinctive names drawn from North Carolina voter rolls. They asked GPT-3.5 and GPT-4 to rank them for jobs across a thousand trials. *If the model were unbiased, each demographic group would top-rank twelve and a half percent of the time.*

The result — *some demographic groups topped at eleven percent, others much higher.* Statistically significant bias. *Reproducible. The methodology and data are public on Bloomberg's GitHub.* The lesson — *AI inherits biases from the training data. If you're using it in a context where bias matters, you need explicit controls.*

The personal-data principle. *What you put into an AI tool may be retained, processed, and in some cases used to train future models.* Default consumer accounts often have looser data terms than enterprise accounts.

The simple rule. *Don't put client-confidential, regulated, or personally-sensitive information into a free consumer AI account.* Use the enterprise version your company has licensed — OpenAI Enterprise, Anthropic Claude for Work, Microsoft 365 Copilot. *All of these have training opt-out by default and tenant-isolated data handling.* Read the trust page. Or ask IT.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · EU AI Act]

The regulatory landscape. *EU AI Act — Regulation twenty twenty-four sixteen-eighty-nine — published August twenty twenty-four, in tranches through August second twenty twenty-six.* High-risk AI systems in Annex Three. Transparency obligations under Article Fifty — *if you're interacting with AI, the AI must say so.* Fines under Article Ninety-Nine — up to fifteen million euros or three percent of global turnover.

For your job — *if your company operates in the EU or serves EU customers, the AI Act applies even if you're outside the EU.* This is the framework regulators will use globally over the next few years.
