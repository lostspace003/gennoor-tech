The detection trap.

The first instinct when AI arrived in classrooms. *Build detection tools. Catch AI-generated work. Punish students.*

What happened in practice. *AI detection tools have unacceptable false-positive rates.* Students with English as a second language, students with learning differences, students with naturally unusual writing patterns — *disproportionately flagged.*

The discipline. *Detection alone is not a foundation for academic integrity in the AI era.* It's part of a broader strategy at best.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 integrity pillars]

Three pillars of AI-era academic integrity.

*Pillar one — assessment redesign.* Some assessments are AI-resistant by design. In-class work. Conversations with the student. Collaborative problem-solving. Defending the work verbally.

*Pillar two — explicit policy.* Honour codes updated. *Where is AI permitted? Where is it not? With what disclosure?* Clear at the start of every course.

*Pillar three — ongoing conversation.* Academic integrity is not a one-time signature on day one. *Ongoing dialogue throughout the term.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the disclosure model]

The disclosure model.

A model that works in higher education and increasingly in high school.

*Tier one — AI prohibited.* Specific assessments where original work is required. In-class essays. Defended oral exams. *Clearly labelled.*

*Tier two — AI permitted with disclosure.* Drafts, brainstorming, editing support. *Students disclose what they used, how, and why.* Disclosure is graded.

*Tier three — AI required.* Some courses train students to use AI effectively. *Use is part of the learning objective.*

What works. *Tier labels on every assessment.* What doesn't work. *Blanket policy that ignores context.*
