Three reasons AI literacy must be a discipline.

*Reason one — students already use AI.* Whether the school teaches it or not. *Better that they learn to use it well.*

*Reason two — workplace expectation.* By 2030, AI fluency expected in most knowledge-work roles. *Schools either prepare students or they don't.*

*Reason three — civic responsibility.* Voting, consuming media, evaluating claims. *AI literacy is increasingly civic literacy.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 components of AI literacy]

Four components of an AI literacy curriculum.

*Component one — what AI is and is not.* Pattern matching at scale. Not reasoning in the human sense. Not consciousness.

*Component two — where AI works and where it doesn't.* Tasks AI does well — summarising, drafting, classifying. Tasks AI struggles with — novel problems, current events, judgement.

*Component three — verification.* How to check AI output. Cross-reference with authoritative sources. *Never trust without verification on consequential questions.*

*Component four — ethics.* Bias in training data. Environmental cost. Labour displacement. Surveillance. *Discussed openly with students, not avoided.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · age-appropriate sequencing]

Age-appropriate sequencing.

*Elementary.* Concept-level. "AI is a tool. It can be helpful. It can be wrong." Hands-on with carefully scoped tools.

*Middle school.* Mechanics-level. Pattern matching. Training data. Limitations. Verification practices.

*High school.* Critical-thinking level. Bias. Ethics. Career implications. Independent use with judgement.

*Higher education.* Discipline-specific. Each major addresses AI's role in that field — what works, what doesn't, what the discipline's professionals owe society.
