# AI in Education — Research Dossier

**Course slug:** `ai-in-education`
**Track:** Industry · Education
**Audience:** Superintendents, heads of school, provosts, deans, curriculum directors, IT and academic affairs leaders, teachers and faculty who own AI deployment at the classroom level
**Length:** 8 chapters · ~36 min total
**Voice:** Andrew (`en-US-AndrewMultilingualNeural`)

## Thesis

AI in education succeeds when it augments teachers and supports learners — not when it replaces either. The 2023–26 wave is not about smarter models in the classroom; it is about the *operating discipline* schools and universities need to ship AI without breaking pedagogy, integrity, equity, or child-safety law. Six plays that work (literacy as a discipline · tutoring + writing partner · educator workflow · integrity · equity · privacy + safety) and three anti-plays that quietly destroy trust (replacing teachers · surveillance-heavy AI · autonomous high-stakes grading). The pedagogy-first filter — teachers and instructional designers in the room from day one — is the difference between adoption and rejection.

## Named incidents

1. **NYC DOE ChatGPT ban + reversal (Jan→May 2023)** — the canonical "ban then integrate" arc; ground the integrity chapter, not the tutoring chapter.
2. **Turnitin AI detector reliability concerns (2023–24)** — Vanderbilt and Michigan State publicly disabled it; ground the detection-trap argument.
3. **Stanford detector-bias study (Liang et al. 2023)** — AI detectors flag non-native-English writing at higher false-positive rates; ground the equity-risk argument.
4. **The confabulated-citation pattern** — student essays citing papers that do not exist. Industry-observed; ground in cross-domain analog (Mata v. Avianca S.D.N.Y. Jun 2023).

## What we do NOT say

- **No "AI replaces teachers" framing.** Teacher + AI beats either alone. Augment, do not substitute.
- **No "AI tutoring solves Bloom's 2-sigma."** Bloom is the benchmark, not the floor we have cleared.
- **No detector-only integrity strategy.** Detection has documented false-positive bias; design + disclosure beats detection.
- **No vendor preference.** Khanmigo, Duolingo Max, Canvas, Google Classroom, Schoology, Blackboard, Moodle referenced even-handedly.
- **No autonomous high-stakes grading.** Formative feedback yes; summative grading without teacher-in-the-loop, no.
- **No surveillance framing.** Proctoring + behavioural monitoring is the anti-play.
- **No invented citations.** When pointing at a study or court case, name it precisely (Liang et al., Mata v. Avianca) or reframe as industry pattern.

## Chapter blueprint

### Chapter 1 — The AI in education landscape (~5 min)
**What:** Stanford AI Index 2025 policy-issuance curve. Khanmigo, Duolingo Max, LMS AI features as the live landscape. Six plays (literacy · tutoring · educators · integrity · equity · privacy) and three anti-plays (replace teachers · surveillance · autonomous grading). The pedagogy-first filter: teachers, instructional designers, AI specialists in the room from day one.
**Sources:** [1], [2], [3], [4], [11].

### Chapter 2 — AI literacy as a discipline (~5 min)
**What:** Three reasons literacy is core (workforce · civic · safety). Four components: what AI is · where it works and fails · verification habits · ethics. Age-appropriate sequencing from elementary (concepts + supervised use) through middle/high (verification + integrity) to higher ed (domain integration). OECD AIFS + UNESCO framing.
**Sources:** [5], [10].

### Chapter 3 — AI tutoring and writing partner (~5 min)
**What:** Three working use cases (Socratic tutoring · writing feedback · language practice — Khanmigo + Duolingo Max patterns). Three disappointments (hallucinated explanations · over-help · weak in advanced math/proof). Three design principles: supplement not substitute · bounded by teacher · explicit on limitations. Bloom's 2-sigma is the benchmark, not the claim.
**Sources:** [2], [3], [20], [22].

### Chapter 4 — AI for educators (~4 min)
**What:** Four use cases that return time to teaching: lesson planning · differentiation · feedback drafting · admin/communications. The grading line: AI for formative feedback yes; summative grading without teacher-in-the-loop no. Three failure modes: homogenisation (every lesson sounds the same) · feedback fatigue (volume up, signal down) · privacy creep (student data in non-FERPA-aligned tools).
**Sources:** [4], [15].

### Chapter 5 — Academic integrity in the AI era (~5 min)
**What:** The detection trap — Turnitin reliability concerns + Liang et al. bias against non-native English. Three integrity pillars: assessment redesign · disclosure norms · process evidence (drafts, version history). Three-tier disclosure model: prohibited · permitted with disclosure · required. NYC DOE arc as the cautionary tale on ban-first policy. The confabulated-citation pattern — cross-domain analog Mata v. Avianca.
**Sources:** [11], [12], [13], [22].

### Chapter 6 — Equity and access (~4 min)
**What:** The two-direction reality — AI can narrow gaps (tutoring scale · translation · accessibility) or widen them (premium-tier divide · model bias · surveillance disparity). Three equity benefits: 1:1 tutoring at scale · multilingual access · accommodations. Three equity risks: premium divide · bias in outputs and detectors · surveillance applied unevenly by school demographics.
**Sources:** [13], [14], [16].

### Chapter 7 — Privacy and safety (~5 min)
**What:** Four regulatory frames: FERPA (US records) · COPPA (US under-13) · GDPR Art 8 + UK Children's Code (EU/UK) · DPDPA 2023 (India). Three contract essentials: school-official designation · no-training-on-student-data clause · deletion + audit rights. Child-safety guardrails: content filtering · age-gating · Common Sense Media + EdSAFE SAFE benchmarks as procurement input. Student Privacy Pledge as baseline vendor screen.
**Sources:** [6], [7], [8], [9], [16], [17], [18], [19].

### Chapter 8 — Making it stick · 12-month rollout (~3 min)
**What:** Months 1-3: pedagogy-first committee + literacy curriculum + acceptable-use policy + vendor screen. Months 4-6: 2 pilots (1 tutoring, 1 educator). Months 7-9: integrity + disclosure rollout. Months 10-12: equity + privacy audit + scale decision. Four trust trip-wires: no surveillance-heavy deployment · no autonomous high-stakes grading · no vendor without FERPA/COPPA/GDPR posture · no detection-only integrity policy. Interactive Markdown builder for superintendent · head of school · provost · dean.
**Sources:** Composite of [5], [15], [17], [18].
