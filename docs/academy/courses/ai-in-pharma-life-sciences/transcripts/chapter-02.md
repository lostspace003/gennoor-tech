> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · what AI changes]

What AI changes in discovery.

*Target identification.* Genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics integrated at scale. AI surfaces candidate targets that traditional approaches miss.

*Lead generation.* Generative chemistry produces candidate molecules with desired properties. Computational screening at scales previously infeasible.

*ADMET prediction.* Absorption, distribution, metabolism, excretion, toxicity predicted from structure before synthesis. Earlier kill decisions on bad candidates.

*Protein engineering.* AlphaFold and successors made structure-based design routine for many target classes. Antibody and biologics design accelerated meaningfully.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the disappointment pattern]

The disappointment pattern. *Important to name.*

Several high-profile AI discovery programmes have produced candidates that progressed to clinical trials. *Not all have outperformed traditionally discovered molecules at the bedside.* Clinical trial failures of AI-originated candidates have happened publicly.

The lesson is not that AI discovery doesn't work. *The lesson is that AI accelerates the early stages. Clinical success still depends on biology, target validation, and patient selection.* AI is a discovery accelerator. *AI is not a guarantee of clinical success.*

Honest communication of this to executives matters. *Vendor-pitch claims of "AI-designed drugs reach market faster" require careful interpretation.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the validation discipline]

The validation discipline in AI discovery.

*Computational predictions require wet-lab validation.* Always. Predicted binding affinity is confirmed by experiment. Predicted ADMET is confirmed by assay.

*Negative controls matter.* If your AI is highly enriching active compounds in retrospective benchmarks, ensure it is on diverse held-out sets, not familiar training data. *Otherwise enrichment is illusion.*

*External benchmarking helps.* Public competitions and consortium benchmarks expose what actually works versus what works only on internal data.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score discovery AI honestly. *Two questions.*

*Question one — for AI-identified candidates that progressed to wet-lab validation, what's the confirmation rate?* High enrichment over random green. Modest enrichment amber. *No enrichment after validation red — model is over-fit or measuring wrong thing.*

*Question two — what cycle-time reduction has AI delivered for early discovery stages?* Three to twelve months faster green. *Marginal reduction amber. No reduction red — not paying back the investment.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pull one AI discovery project from the last year.* What did the wet-lab confirmation rate look like? *That's your honest signal, not the vendor's enrichment claims.*
