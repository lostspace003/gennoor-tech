Major pharma companies receive significant adverse event report volume daily. *Spontaneous reports from healthcare professionals and consumers. Literature reports. Regulatory authority transfers.*

Three pharmacovigilance AI use cases.

*Use case one — case intake and coding.* AI extracts adverse event details from unstructured reports. MedDRA coding. Patient demographics. Causality factors.

*Use case two — signal detection.* AI surfaces statistical signals from PV databases that warrant medical review.

*Use case three — literature surveillance.* AI scans medical literature for case reports relevant to a product's safety profile.

Medical review preserved. *Non-negotiable.*

AI accelerates case processing. *Medical review of serious cases remains the qualified medical officer's responsibility.* Causality assessment remains human. Periodic safety report sign-off remains human.

The regulatory expectation. *FDA, EMA, and other authorities expect a qualified safety physician's judgement on causality and signal evaluation.* AI cannot replace that judgement. AI surfaces, prioritises, and prepares the work.

The Mata cross-domain. *AI-generated causality assessments may be plausible but wrong.* Without human verification of source documentation, errors enter periodic safety reports. *Eventually regulators find them.*

Real-world evidence. *Growing AI role.*

RWE is increasingly used in post-market commitments, label expansions, and reimbursement submissions. *FDA and EMA both have RWE frameworks.* AI analyses real-world data sources — EHR, claims, registries — to support these submissions.

The discipline. *Pre-specify the analysis plan before AI sees the data.* Otherwise the AI optimises for outcomes that look favourable rather than outcomes that are true.

What works. *Protocol locked. AI applied as specified. Results reported regardless of direction.*

What does not. *AI run iteratively until a favourable result emerges.* Statistical validity destroyed. Regulator credibility lost.
