> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 components]

Four governance components for public-sector AI.

*Component one — public AI inventory.* Documentation of every AI system in use. *OMB M-twenty-four-ten requires this for US federal agencies. UK, EU, and others have similar regimes.*

*Component two — impact assessment.* Documented analysis of risks to rights, equity, and outcomes. *EU AI Act fundamental rights impact assessment. US AI use case impact assessment.*

*Component three — appeal pathway.* For any adverse decision involving AI, a documented appeal process. *Constitutional due process. Statutory rights.*

*Component four — accountability.* Named responsible official. Documented decision authority. *Not "the system decided."*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the public inventory discipline]

The public inventory discipline.

The pattern. *Every AI use case has an inventory entry.* Description in plain language. Purpose. Risk classification. Impact assessment status. Named accountable official.

What this enables. *Public can know what AI government uses. Oversight bodies can examine. Civil society can hold to account. Other agencies can learn.*

What it requires. *Discipline to maintain.* Inventory goes stale fast unless actively updated. Quarterly review minimum. *Annual reviews allow drift.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · procurement implications]

Procurement implications.

Government AI is often procured from vendors. *Vendor contracts must include specific terms.*

*Term one — explainability and audit rights.* Vendor provides documentation sufficient for impact assessment. Government can audit on reasonable notice.

*Term two — bias and fairness testing.* Vendor demonstrates testing methodology. Government can require additional testing.

*Term three — data handling.* What vendor does with citizen data. *Citizen data not used for vendor commercial purposes without explicit authority.*

*Term four — exit and continuity.* Vendor provides migration support. Records remain accessible to government even if vendor relationship ends.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score governance posture honestly. *Three questions.*

*Question one — public AI inventory current and accurate.* Yes, reviewed in last quarter green. Yes but stale amber. *No public inventory red.*

*Question two — every active AI use case has documented impact assessment.* Yes green. Most amber. *Many missing red.*

*Question three — vendor AI contracts include the four standard terms.* Yes for new contracts green. Mixed amber. *No red.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pull your agency's AI inventory.* Is it complete? Is each entry current? *If you have AI systems running that aren't in the inventory, you have a governance debt to close.*
