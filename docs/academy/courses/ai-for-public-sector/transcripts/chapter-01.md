The principle that runs through this course. *In government, AI must serve the public — and be accountable to it.* Citizens are not customers. *They cannot easily switch suppliers. They cannot easily exit the relationship. They cannot easily refuse the service.*

That changes everything. *Accountability, transparency, due process, and equal treatment are not optional. They are the constraint within which government AI operates.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 5 plays that ship]

Five public-sector AI plays that consistently ship value.

*Play one — service delivery automation.* Self-service applications, status checking, simple eligibility queries.

*Play two — case work augmentation.* Document review, summarisation, evidence preparation for case workers. *Humans decide. AI prepares.*

*Play three — fraud detection in benefits and procurement.* Pattern detection on transactions and applications.

*Play four — intelligent search across government records.* Internal staff finding relevant precedent, regulation, prior decisions.

*Play five — translation and accessibility.* Making services available in multiple languages and accessible formats.

These five ship. *The discipline is what separates good implementation from headline-making failure.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 patterns that fail]

Three patterns that fail in public-sector AI.

*Failure pattern one — algorithmic decisions affecting rights without human review.* Benefits denials, immigration adjudications, child welfare decisions, criminal justice scoring. *These are subject to constitutional and statutory due process. AI alone cannot decide.*

*Failure pattern two — chatbots representing the government without verification.* The Air Canada Moffatt cross-domain applies directly. *Government chatbot mistakes become government commitments. Government chatbot misrepresentations become Federal Register issues.*

*Failure pattern three — opaque AI in high-stakes contexts.* If the citizen cannot understand why a decision was made, they cannot appeal it. *Black-box AI in high-stakes public-sector contexts breaks due process. Period.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the regulatory frame]

The regulatory frame.

*United States.* OMB Memorandum M-twenty-four-ten — government use of AI. Agency requirements for impact assessments, public inventory, accountability. *State and local separate.*

*Europe.* EU AI Act Article six and Annex three — many public-sector uses are classified high-risk. Fundamental rights impact assessments required.

*United Kingdom.* Government Digital Service standards. The Algorithmic Transparency Recording Standard.

*India.* DPDPA twenty twenty-three. Specific obligations on government as data fiduciary.

*Common.* Government AI is more constrained than commercial AI. *And rightly so.*
