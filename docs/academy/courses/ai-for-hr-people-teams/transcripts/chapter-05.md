Chapter five. *Candidate experience and the three lines we don't cross.* The use case with the highest reputational risk in HR AI. *Anything the AI says to a candidate becomes the company's statement.*

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · Talent Board data]

The candidate-experience data. *Talent Board CandE twenty twenty-four survey — twenty-nine percent of candidates never heard back.* Forty-eight percent were unhappy with feedback they did receive.

That's the gap AI is positioned to fill. *Automated status updates. Personalised rejection feedback. Faster turnaround.* All real productivity gains for candidates and for HR teams.

The risk. *Generic AI-generated feedback worse than no feedback.* Inconsistent voice. Wrong information. Inadvertent commitments. *The Air Canada Moffatt cross-domain analog applies directly* — if your AI tells a candidate they got the role and they didn't, that's a problem.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · disclosure]

The three-sentence disclosure pattern. *Same as the support-bots course.*

Sentence one — *"Hi, I'm Acme Recruiting Assistant, an AI helper."* Direct. Named. Categorised.

Sentence two — *"I can help with application status, scheduling, and general questions about the role."* Scope-bound.

Sentence three — *"For anything I can't answer, I'll connect you to a human recruiter."* Escalation promise upfront.

EU AI Act Article Fifty makes this mandatory from August second twenty twenty-six. *Pew Research twenty twenty-five — seventy percent of candidates prefer disclosure.* Aligned with consumer preference.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:10]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the 3 lines]

The three lines we don't cross. *Memorise them.*

*Line one — no AI ratings.* No numerical AI-generated score on the candidate. No grade. No quality rank. *Humans rate. AI doesn't.*

*Line two — no AI stack ranking.* No automated ordering of candidates by AI-derived score. *Mobley v. Workday applies directly here.* Vendor-as-agent doctrine. The minute the AI ranks candidates without human review, you've created an enforcement target.

*Line three — no flight-risk prediction.* No AI inference about whether an employee is about to leave. *This is the most-pitched and least-defensible application of AI in HR analytics.* The combination of surveillance feel, predictive inaccuracy, and legal exposure makes it not worth the productivity gain.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:15]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the discipline]

The discipline. *Inside the three lines, there's a lot of legitimate ground.*

Status updates. Interview scheduling. *Personalised rejection feedback with human review.* Onboarding workflows. L&D personalisation. Skills-gap analysis at the role level, not the individual level.

*Outside the three lines — the regulator is waiting.* And the works council. And the consumer-protection litigator.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

Verify nothing in them is a binding commitment, a discriminatory implication, or a fabricated detail.* If any is — fix the controls before next quarter.
