The week-4 cliff.

The pattern most internal copilots hit. *Week 1-3 enthusiasm. Week 4-5 dropoff.*

Cause. *Initial excitement fades. Users hit failure modes. They try Ctrl+F again, find faster results, and drift back to the old habit.*

The discipline. *Plan adoption interventions specifically for weeks 4 through 8.* Most teams forget about this and treat adoption as a launch concern only.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 adoption interventions]

Four adoption interventions this team ran.

*Intervention one — weekly tip emails.* "This week try asking the copilot X." Short. Practical. Sent to active users.

*Intervention two — manager toolkits.* Managers got a playbook on how to integrate the copilot into team rituals. Onboarding handover · 1:1 prep · benefits enrollment season.

*Intervention three — feature releases as engagement points.* Every 2 weeks, a new capability shipped. Communicated. *Gave users a reason to come back.*

*Intervention four — failure-mode transparency.* When the copilot failed users, HR ops manager publicly acknowledged in town hall. *Trust through honesty.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 adoption signals]

Three adoption signals that predicted long-term success.

*Signal one — repeat usage.* Users coming back within 7 days. *Week 1: 70%. Week 4: 55%. Week 8: 50% sustained. Healthy.*

*Signal two — question diversity.* Range of question types users ask. *Started narrow (just onboarding). Widened over 8 weeks (benefits, leave, expenses, policies).* Diversity proves users trust the bot for more.

*Signal three — manager endorsement.* Managers actively referring team members. *Tracked via question source. Manager-referred users had 3x higher retention.*

These three are leading indicators. *They predict month-three retention better than week-one satisfaction.*
