The principle. *Two use cases. Two quarters. One regulatory posture.* Same discipline as every function in this academy. *Pick the right two for your BFSI sub-sector. Don't deploy across credit, fraud, insurance, markets, customer-facing all at once.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

*Quarter one — the lower-risk use case.* For banks, that's typically *fraud and AML augmentation* or *customer-facing assistance with strong guardrails.* For insurance — *claims AI with quad-eye* on settlements. For asset management — *research summarisation with quote-or-cut.* These are the use cases where AI delivers value without high-risk regulatory exposure.

*Quarter two — one high-risk use case with full controls.* For banks — *one underwriting or pricing AI model with EU AI Act plus SR Eleven-Seven plus CFPB controls.* For insurance — *one pricing or underwriting model with same controls.* This is where you start touching Article Six through Twenty-Nine obligations.

*Defer.* Anything that touches multiple regulators simultaneously. Anything that creates customer remediation exposure. *Year two — if the framework holds.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · trust trip-wires]

Four trust trip-wires. *Hard nos.*

*One — no high-risk model without SR Eleven-Seven plus EU AI Act plus NIST controls.* Independent validation. Bias testing. Audit trail. *Non-negotiable.*

*Two — no customer-facing AI without disclosure.* EU AI Act Article Fifty. *UK FCA Consumer Duty.* The Air Canada precedent.

*Three — no AI-generated specific facts in regulated content unverified.* SAR filings. Research notes. Customer communications. *Quote-or-cut. The Mata principle.*

*Four — no "set and forget."* Quarterly model monitoring. Annual independent validation. *Examiner-ready documentation.*
