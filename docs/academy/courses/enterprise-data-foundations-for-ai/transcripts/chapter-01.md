The principle that runs through this course. *Most AI projects fail at the data step.* Not at the model step. Not at the use case step. *At the data step.*

Survey after survey from DAMA, McKinsey, BCG/MIT — *the consistent pattern is that organisations underestimate the data work required for AI initiatives.* They start with the model. They discover the data isn't ready. They pause. *They lose six to twelve months.*

The honest version. *Get the data foundations right. The AI work that comes after is the easier half.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 5 pillars]

Five pillars of data foundations.

*Pillar one — quality.* Accuracy, completeness, freshness, consistency.

*Pillar two — lineage.* Provenance you can defend to an auditor.

*Pillar three — governance.* Ownership, access, retention, deletion — GDPR plus DPDPA plus CCPA enforceable.

*Pillar four — privacy.* Masking, tokenisation, differential privacy where the data is sensitive.

*Pillar five — platform pattern.* The architectural choices — lakehouse, data products, contracts, mesh — that make all of the above possible at scale.

Most enterprises are amber to red across multiple pillars. *That's not failure. That's the starting position.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 anti-patterns]

Three anti-patterns I see consistently.

*Anti-pattern one — buy a platform without fixing the data.* Modern data platforms are impressive. *They don't fix bad data. They just store it more expensively.*

*Anti-pattern two — clean the lake instead of building data products.* Massive cleanup projects that boil the ocean. *Six months later, partial progress, no consumable outcome.* The data product pattern delivers value early and compounds.

*Anti-pattern three — separate "data team" disconnected from business.* Data engineers building infrastructure that the business never asked for. *No outcomes. Eventually defunded.*
