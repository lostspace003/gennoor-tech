# AI Talent Strategy — Research Dossier

**Course slug:** `ai-talent-strategy`
**Track:** Leadership · HR & Talent
**Audience:** CHROs, heads of talent, CIOs/CTOs co-owning AI capability-building
**Length:** 8 chapters · ~36 min total
**Voice:** Emma (`en-GB-EmmaMultilingualNeural`)

## Thesis

AI talent — not the technology — is the bottleneck. Frontier models are broadly accessible; the people who can apply them to a real P&L, ship them safely, and operate them for years are genuinely scarce. Scarcity is also asymmetric: senior ML/research specialists clear $300-500K+ in major markets while applied practitioners and AI-fluent domain professionals are far more buildable. The CHRO move is not to out-bid Big Tech for principals; it is to segment the talent stack, convert internals, hire at senior (not principal), and treat AI fluency as a 5-10x leverage move over hiring. This course teaches the CHRO/CIO duo how to build that stack honestly — without the comp arms race, the title inflation, or the vendor-only shortcut.

## Named incidents

1. **iTutorGroup EEOC settlement (Aug 2023)** — $365K for AI-driven age discrimination in recruiting. Ground "vendor doesn't absorb your Title VII liability."
2. **Mobley v. Workday (N.D. Cal., 2024-25)** — class action over AI screening disparate impact, allowed to proceed. Ground "hiring AI is on the buyer's risk surface."
3. **NYC Local Law 144 (2023-24)** — first bias-audit-and-notice regime for automated employment decision tools. Ground operational compliance.
4. **The resume-confabulation pattern (2024-26)** — AI-assisted CVs with invented employers. Ground in cross-domain analog (Mata v. Avianca) rather than a named hiring incident.

## What we do NOT say

- **No "AI will replace HR" framing.** AI augments talent decisions; the CHRO judgement layer is the accountability layer.
- **No comp-arms-race advice.** Out-bidding Big Tech for principals is not the strategy; segment + convert + senior-not-principal is.
- **No vendor selection bias.** Workday, SAP SuccessFactors, Oracle, Eightfold, Gloat referenced even-handedly.
- **No "hire your way out of it."** Fluency build is 5-10x leverage; we say so.
- **No invented case studies.** Where we cite a specific company/$, it's WEF/McKinsey/BCG/EEOC/Stanford-grade or reframed as industry pattern.
- **No EU AI Act misreads.** Annex III §4 covers employment use; demand-forecasting-style HR analytics may not be — we draw the line.

## Chapter blueprint

### Chapter 1 — The talent landscape (~5 min)
**What:** WEF 86% / 39% / 59%. McKinsey 78% adoption + talent named #1 barrier. Stanford AI Index — 90% of notable models from industry. Three segments (senior specialist, applied practitioner, AI-fluent domain) with different markets, economics, leverage. Three failure modes: hire-only, train-only, vendor-only.
**Sources:** [1], [2], [3], [4], [7].

### Chapter 2 — Build, buy, borrow (~5 min)
**What:** Three modes with three signals each. 2026 cost reality from comp trackers — $300-500K+ senior ML, $600K-$1M+ principal/research at frontier labs. Build = convert internals via fluency + apprenticeship. Buy = senior (not principal) + adjacent fields. Borrow = vendors, contractors, fractional. The honest math: build/borrow beats buy for ~80% of org needs.
**Sources:** [6], [19], [20].

### Chapter 3 — Role architecture (~5 min)
**What:** Five core AI roles (ML engineer, applied scientist, ML platform/MLOps, product/AI PM, AI lead). Three emerging (prompt/agent engineer, AI ethics/governance lead, AI-fluent domain owner). The reporting question — CIO vs CTO vs CEO direct — anchored on whether AI is infrastructure, product, or strategy. Skills inference layer from HR-tech (Workday, Eightfold, Gloat).
**Sources:** [8], [9].

### Chapter 4 — Hiring patterns (~5 min)
**What:** Three sourcing patterns: convert internals (cheapest), hire at senior not principal (highest ROI), hire from adjacent fields (physics, stats, software). Three evaluation principles: real-problem work sample > LeetCode, judgement > tools, ship-history > paper-pedigree. Three anti-patterns: pay-only, title-inflation, eternal-loop interviewing. The Title VII overlay — EEOC + OFCCP recordkeeping applies to AI selection tools too.
**Sources:** [11], [15], [16], [22].

### Chapter 5 — Retention (~5 min)
**What:** Four drivers in priority order: (1) interesting work, (2) resources + compute, (3) leadership credibility on AI, (4) comp at market median+. The 3-12 month flight-risk window — regret-attrition concentrates here. Three failure modes: comp-only retention, no career ladder for ICs, leadership that doesn't ship. The honest line: above market median, money stops mattering.
**Sources:** [17].

### Chapter 6 — The AI fluency build (~5 min)
**What:** Fluency is 5-10x payback vs senior hires (top-decile pattern). Three tiers: universal (5-10 hr for everyone), functional (20-40 hr for knowledge-worker functions), power users (60-120 hr for builders/champions). Three patterns that work: leadership-first cascade, real-work projects (not labs), measure adoption not completion. Platforms: Coursera, Pluralsight, O'Reilly, Microsoft Learn, AWS Skill Builder.
**Sources:** [5], [10], [20].

### Chapter 7 — Organisational design (~5 min)
**What:** Three org models: centralised CoE (good <200 ppl), federated (good for ~Big Tech maturity), hub-and-spoke (dominant 500+ ppl). Three design questions: who owns the platform, who owns the use cases, who owns risk + governance. Three failure modes: shadow-AI proliferation, central bottleneck, no risk-owner. The EU AI Act + Colorado SB24-205 + NYC LL144 overlay — employment AI is Annex III high-risk.
**Sources:** [12], [13], [14], [18].

### Chapter 8 — Making it stick · 12-month talent roadmap (~6 min)
**What:** 12-month rollout. Months 1-3: segment talent, name AI lead, baseline fluency. Months 4-6: convert 5-10 internals, hire 2-3 seniors, ship fluency tier-1. Months 7-9: hub-and-spoke stand-up, bias-audit on any AI hiring tool (NYC LL144 + EEOC). Months 10-12: retention review, fluency tier-2 + tier-3, board update. Four trust trip-wires: no AI hiring tool without bias audit, no principal hire without ship-history, no fluency programme without leadership cascade, no AI lead without resourcing authority. **Sources:** Composite of [11], [12], [14], [17], [18].
