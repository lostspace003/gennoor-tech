> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 sourcing patterns]

Three sourcing patterns that work.

*Pattern one — convert internal engineers to AI engineers.* The strongest software engineer in your org can become a credible AI engineer with three to six months of focused investment. *Faster and cheaper than external hire. Lower flight risk.*

*Pattern two — hire at the senior-engineer not principal level.* The market for principals is brutal. The market for strong senior engineers willing to grow is workable. *Hire there, develop into principal over eighteen months.*

*Pattern three — hire from adjacent fields.* Strong physicists, computational biologists, quantitative analysts often transition into ML well. *Broader pool. Often cheaper. Comparable performance.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 evaluation principles]

Three AI hiring evaluation principles.

*Principle one — practical demo over theoretical interview.* Give the candidate a realistic problem with realistic data and watch them work. *More signal than abstract algorithms questions.*

*Principle two — fit with your specific stack matters.* PyTorch versus TensorFlow versus JAX. Lakehouse versus warehouse. Cloud A versus cloud B. *Pure ML talent doesn't transfer perfectly across stacks. Hire for stack fit when you can.*

*Principle three — softer skills compound.* Can they write a clear design doc? Can they explain to a non-technical stakeholder? *Brilliant ML engineers who can't communicate become bottlenecks instead of leverage.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 anti-patterns]

Three hiring anti-patterns.

*Anti-pattern one — pay-only competition.* You'll lose. *Big tech and well-funded startups will outbid you. Compete on something other than just total comp.*

*Anti-pattern two — fancy-title-as-substitute.* Promote engineers to "Head of AI" without authority or capability. *Title inflation. Real candidates see through this.*

*Anti-pattern three — eternal interview loops.* Eight rounds of interviews over six weeks. The candidate accepts elsewhere by week three. *Streamline to three to four rounds within ten business days.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score hiring honestly. *Two questions.*

*Question one — what's your AI engineer offer-to-acceptance rate?* Above sixty percent is green. Forty to sixty amber. *Under forty percent red — your hiring process or comp package needs work.*

*Question two — what's your time from first interview to offer?* Under ten business days green. Two to three weeks amber. *More than three weeks red.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pull your last five AI hiring loops that didn't end in an offer accepted.* Diagnose why each fell apart. *Pattern emerges. Fix the most common cause first.*
