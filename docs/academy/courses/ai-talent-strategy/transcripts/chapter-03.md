> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 5 core roles]

Five core AI roles in 2026.

*Role one — AI/ML engineer.* Builds and deploys models. Most common AI hiring need.

*Role two — data scientist.* Analyses data, frames problems, prototypes. Often confused with ML engineer. *Different skill set.*

*Role three — MLOps engineer.* Operates AI in production. The handoff target. *Most under-hired role.*

*Role four — AI product manager.* Translates business need to AI capability. Owns measurement. *The AI-specific PM is a real specialisation.*

*Role five — AI strategist or AI lead.* Senior role. Owns portfolio direction. Bridges to executives.

These are the five core roles. *Most orgs have one and three. They under-staff two, four, and five. That's the gap.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 emerging roles]

Three emerging roles in 2026.

*Emerging role one — AI governance lead.* Owns AI inventory, risk register, compliance liaison. *Increasingly required by EU AI Act and ISO forty-two thousand one.*

*Emerging role two — prompt engineer or AI workflow designer.* Specialises in eliciting reliable behaviour from foundation models. *Real specialisation. Will mature into broader AI integration roles.*

*Emerging role three — AI ethicist.* Less common but appearing in regulated industries. *Healthcare, financial services, government.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the leadership question]

The leadership question. *AI lead role.*

In most enterprises in 2026, *the AI lead reports to one of three places.* CIO — most common. *Tech-centric, infrastructure focus.* CTO — when AI is core to product. *Product-centric, build focus.* CEO direct — when AI is strategic priority. *Cross-functional, strategy focus.*

Each has tradeoffs. *Wrong reporting line is the single highest-cost AI talent decision an organisation makes.* Get this right at hire time. *Rebooting reporting structure after the fact is hard.*
