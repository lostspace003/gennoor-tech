> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the principle]

The principle that runs through this course. *AI talent is the bottleneck. Not the technology.*

The technology is broadly accessible. *The talent to apply it to real business problems — to build the right thing, to ship it safely, to operate it for years — is genuinely scarce.* And scarcity is asymmetric. *The talent goes to the orgs that can attract and keep them.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 segments of AI talent]

Three segments of AI talent. *Three different markets.*

*Segment one — senior ML and AI specialists.* PhDs and senior engineers who can build novel models. *Very scarce. Globally mobile. Expensive.*

*Segment two — applied ML and MLOps practitioners.* Mid-level engineers who can deploy and operate AI in production. *Scarce. Increasingly trained in non-traditional paths.*

*Segment three — AI-augmented domain professionals.* The accountant, the analyst, the marketer who uses AI to multiply their output. *The largest segment. The most under-invested.*

Most orgs over-invest in segment one and under-invest in segment three. *Segment three is where the leverage is.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 failure modes]

Three talent failure modes I see consistently.

*Failure mode one — hire-only strategy.* Try to hire your way out of every AI capability gap. *Senior talent market is brutal. You will lose this game.*

*Failure mode two — train-only strategy.* Try to train your existing people into AI capabilities. *Some can. Many can't. Pretending all can wastes time.*

*Failure mode three — vendor-only strategy.* Outsource everything to consultants and vendors. *Cheaper in year one. Catastrophic in year three when you've built no institutional muscle.*

The working strategy combines all three. *Build, buy, borrow — proportional to what each capability needs.*
