The principle that runs through this course. *In energy and utilities, AI must augment operational reliability, not threaten it.* Grid stability and human safety are not negotiable. *Every AI decision is filtered through that constraint.*

This is different from most enterprise AI. *In retail, AI getting it wrong loses a sale. In utilities, AI getting it wrong can blackout a city or kill a worker. Reliability is the through-line.*

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 5 high-value AI plays]

Five high-value AI plays in energy and utilities.

*Play one — demand forecasting.* Short-term load forecasting at scale. Standard practice in 2026.

*Play two — grid management.* Asset health, congestion management, outage response. *Augmenting human grid operators.*

*Play three — generation optimisation.* For renewables — wind output forecasting, solar yield prediction, battery dispatch. *Critical as the grid decarbonises.*

*Play four — predictive maintenance for transmission and distribution assets.* Transformers, switchgear, lines. *Tens of millions in deferred capital.*

*Play five — customer operations.* Outage notifications, bill explanations, energy advice. *Lowest-stakes AI but highest-volume contact.*

The regulatory frame that shapes every AI decision.

*United States.* NERC reliability standards. FERC orders. State public utility commission oversight on customer-facing decisions.

*Europe.* EU AI Act — energy operators are critical infrastructure under Annex three. Network Code on Cybersecurity from twenty twenty-four. National regulator oversight.

*Asia-Pacific.* National regulatory regimes. Cross-border restrictions on data flows for grid security.

*Common across regions.* AI-driven decisions affecting reliability or customer disconnection are regulator-watched. *Document the human override path before deployment.*
