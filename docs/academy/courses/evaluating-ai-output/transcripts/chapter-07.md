## Transcript

> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Last chapter. *The one-page verification playbook.* The whole course locked into a single document you print, pin, and use. *Not a recap. Not a quiz. An artefact you take to your desk.*

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:20]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · why a playbook]

Why a playbook. *Because by week three, you won't remember the three hallucination patterns by name.* You won't remember overprecision. You won't remember the seventy-two percent fake-citation finding from the Nature paper. *You'll remember the gist. The gist isn't enough.*

A one-page playbook externalises the decisions you've made in this course — *the three checks you'll always run*, the *one escalation rule* for when to stop and ask for help, and the *Friday review*. Pin it. Reference it for nine to ten weeks until the behaviour becomes automatic. *That's what Lally's research says it takes.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:25]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · three checks]

Section one of the playbook. *Your three never-skip checks.* Pick from the patterns you learned. *Confident fabrication.* *Plausible detail.* *Stale fact.* *Citation existence.* *Citation accuracy.* *Demographic-correlated criteria.* *Regional defaults.* *Tone bias.*

Three of these — *for your role, your typical output type, your stake level.* Don't pick eight. Three. *The reason most evaluation training fails is that the checklist gets too long and stops getting used.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · escalation]

Section two. *Your one escalation rule.* The condition under which you stop and ask for human help instead of trusting the AI output. *"If the output touches customer financial data, I escalate to my manager before sending."* *"If the citation is to a foreign-jurisdiction court case, I escalate to legal."* *"If the model contradicts what I thought I knew, I escalate to a subject-matter expert."*

One rule. Specific. Always.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:55]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · friday review]

Section three. *The Friday fifteen-minute review.* Same idea as the Friday review from Course Two and Course Three. *What did I check that worked? What did I skip that I shouldn't have? What got past me?* The Friday review is what turns Monday's verification habit into a calibrated muscle by month three.

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · the builder]

The interactive on screen. Fill in your role, your three never-skip checks, and your one escalation rule. *Click download.* You get a one-page markdown file ready to print. *Save it. Print it. Tape it next to your monitor.*

> [pause for hands-on · ≈ 12 seconds]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 2 · the pin]

The trick — *actually pinning it physically.* Not in OneNote. Not in your bookmarks. *Printed. Visible. Friction-free to glance at.* The Lally research is unambiguous on this — anything that requires opening another app is a habit that doesn't survive week three.

> [S7 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:25]

> [S7 ▸ reveal 1 · the course in one sentence]

The whole course in one sentence. *AI output looks more reliable than it is. The fluency lies. Hallucinations come in three patterns — fabrication, detail, stale. Citations are where the cost gets real — over thirteen hundred court cases globally. Bias hides in business outputs as demographic-correlated criteria or regional defaults. The verification habit takes nine to ten weeks to stick. The playbook is what bridges it.*

Everything else is detail. *That's the whole course.*

> [S7 ▸ reveal 2 · imperative]

What you do today. *Open the builder.* Fill it in for your role. Print it. *Pin it.* The verification habit you build with this playbook is the difference between AI as a productivity tool and AI as a liability vector.

*That's how a forty-five-minute course turns into a verification discipline that protects your work for the next year.* See you in the next one.
