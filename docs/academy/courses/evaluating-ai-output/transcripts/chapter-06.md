Chapter six. Knowing every pattern from the last five chapters means nothing if you don't actually run the check. *Most evaluation training fails not because people forget what to do — they fail because the new behaviour drops off in week three.* This chapter is the behavioural science of *making the verification habit stick*.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:25]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 66 days]

The hard truth first. *Habits take longer than people think.* Phillipa Lally's foundational 2010 study at University College London tracked ninety-six participants for eighty-four days, each adopting a new daily behaviour. *Median time to feel automatic — sixty-six days.* Range — eighteen days to two hundred and fifty-four days.

The implication for verification — *plan a nine to ten week scaffold, not a two-week sprint.* If you're still consciously thinking about the check in week four, that's normal. *That's the slowest two-thirds of the average curve.* Most people quit there. *Don't.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:35]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · overprecision]

The second hard truth. *You're going to feel certain about AI outputs you shouldn't be certain about.* This isn't a moral failing — it's a universal cognitive bias.

Don Moore at UC Berkeley named it *overprecision* — excessive certainty that you know the truth. In his 2008 paper *The Trouble with Overconfidence*, he identified overprecision as the most durable and least understood of the three overconfidence flavours. *Overestimation, overplacement, overprecision.*

The implication. When you read AI output that lands in your area of expertise, *you'll often feel certain it's right* before you've actually checked. That feeling is overprecision. The verification habit exists *because the feeling lies.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:45]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · b equals map]

What actually works. *BJ Fogg from Stanford's Behaviour Design Lab.* His formula is B equals MAP. *Behaviour happens when Motivation, Ability, and a Prompt converge.* If you want a behaviour to stick — make it easy, anchor it to an existing trigger, and celebrate when you do it.

For verification, that translates concretely. *Make the check easy* — a five-minute, three-step list, not a fifteen-minute audit. *Anchor it to an existing trigger* — every time you open an AI tab, you check the source. The trigger is the tab opening, not your willpower. *Celebrate the moment you do it* — a small internal acknowledgement that the check happened. *That's how the neural pattern encodes.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:55]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the routine]

A five-minute verification routine that fits the B-equals-MAP shape. Three steps.

*Step one — name the highest-stakes claim.* In any AI output, identify the *one* claim that, if wrong, would cause the most damage. Don't audit everything. Audit that one.

*Step two — source-check it.* For that one claim — does the source exist, does the quote match, is the date current? Five-minute version of the three-pattern check from chapter three plus the citation check from chapter four.

*Step three — write your verdict.* Two words. *Verified*, *unverified*, or *suspicious*. *Write it down.* Even just at the top of the document where the AI output lives. That written act is the celebration step that encodes the habit.

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:00]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · the designer]

The interactive. Build your verification habit. *Pick your anchor* — the existing trigger your verification habit will attach to. Slack opens. AI tab opens. Before-you-send. *Pick your one always-check* — the type of claim you'll always verify. Names. Numbers. Citations. *Get your personalised B-equals-MAP formula.*

The pattern across what works. *The check has to be five minutes, not fifteen.* The trigger has to be something that already happens. The celebration has to be immediate. Anything that fails one of those three drops off in week three. *Don't fight your brain. Design the habit around how brains actually work.*
