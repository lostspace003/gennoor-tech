Three layers of AI talent readiness.

*Layer one — leadership AI fluency.* Can your executives — CEO, CFO, function heads — read an AI vendor pitch and tell signal from hype? Can they evaluate whether a model is right for a use case? *This is the layer that gets ignored. It's also the one that determines portfolio quality.*

*Layer two — practitioner AI skills.* ML engineers, data scientists, AI product managers, MLOps engineers. *The "build" capability.*

*Layer three — data literacy across functions.* Marketing, finance, HR, operations — can the function leaders read data, ask good questions of it, and use AI-augmented output without losing judgement? *The biggest leverage and the most under-invested layer.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · build/buy/borrow]

The build, buy, borrow framework.

*Build — internal capability development.* Train your existing people. Slower but builds institutional knowledge and retention.

*Buy — hire specialists.* Faster ramp on specific capabilities. *Expensive. And the talent market for senior AI/ML is brutal.*

*Borrow — consultants, contractors, vendor solutions.* Fast and flexible for specific initiatives. *Doesn't build organisational muscle if relied on as the default.*

Most organisations need all three. *The mix depends on maturity stage and what you're trying to achieve.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the leadership fluency gap]

The leadership AI fluency gap. *The most expensive talent gap.*

When executives can't distinguish signal from hype, they fund the wrong initiatives. They believe vendor productivity claims at face value. They under-fund the unsexy data and governance work. *They get talked into the agentic-AI-closes-deals demo and don't ask the hard questions.*

*Fix it deliberately.* AI Foundations courses for the executive team. Quarterly briefings from a credible internal expert. *Required reading lists with substance — not just McKinsey decks.*

A senior team that knows what AI does well and where it breaks is worth more than another ML engineer. *And it's cheaper.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score talent honestly. *Three questions.*

*Question one — leadership.* Can your top five executives each name three things AI is good at and three where it stops being reliable? *Yes for all five is green. Three to four is amber. Less than three is red.*

*Question two — practitioners.* Do you have a named team — with capacity, not just titles — to deliver AI initiatives in the next two quarters? *Yes is green. Mostly is amber. No is red.*

*Question three — data literacy across functions.* In the last quarter, has every function lead asked a data-driven question of an AI tool and acted on the answer? *Yes for most functions is green. Half is amber. Few is red.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pick one executive who you suspect has the weakest AI fluency.* Schedule a thirty-minute one-on-one. Bring three concrete examples of where AI worked in your industry and three where it didn't. *That conversation moves the dial more than another vendor demo.*
