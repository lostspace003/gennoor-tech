> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 pillars]

Three pillars of AI governance readiness.

*Pillar one — risk classification.* Have you classified each AI use case by risk level? *EU AI Act gives you the framework — prohibited, high-risk, limited-risk, minimal-risk.* If your AI portfolio isn't classified, you can't manage it.

*Pillar two — control framework.* Have you mapped NIST AI Risk Management Framework's four functions — govern, map, measure, manage — onto your AI lifecycle? *Or ISO forty-two thousand one twenty twenty-three — the AI management system standard.* Either framework, applied consistently.

*Pillar three — incident response.* If something goes wrong with an AI system publicly, who knows what to do? *Mata v. Avianca cross-domain — a single bad AI output can hit the news. Have a playbook.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 common gaps]

Four governance gaps I see consistently.

*Gap one — no AI inventory.* The org cannot list every AI system in production. *Shadow AI happens. Marketing buys a tool. Customer service implements another. Nobody at the centre knows.*

*Gap two — no risk register at AI initiative level.* The enterprise risk register treats AI as one line item. *Should be one entry per AI initiative with classification, mitigation, monitoring cadence.*

*Gap three — no model lifecycle policy.* When does a model get retrained? Retired? *Most orgs have no answer. Models run for years on stale assumptions.*

*Gap four — no vendor AI clauses.* Standard procurement contracts don't yet include AI-specific protections. *Liability, training data, model updates, sunset rights — these need explicit language.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score governance honestly. *Four questions.*

*Question one — do you have a current inventory of all AI systems in production?* Yes, updated quarterly is green. Yes but stale is amber. *No is red.*

*Question two — has each AI use case been risk-classified using a defensible framework?* Yes is green. Some is amber. No is red.

*Question three — do you have a written incident-response playbook for AI failures?* Yes, tested is green. Yes but untested is amber. No is red.

*Question four — do your vendor contracts include AI-specific terms?* For new contracts yes is green. Mixed is amber. No is red.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · what moves it forward]

What moves governance forward.

*One — name an accountable AI governance lead.* Reports to GC, CISO, or chief risk officer. *Authority to block initiatives that don't meet bar.*

*Two — pick one framework and implement it deeply.* NIST AI RMF or ISO forty-two thousand one. *Pick one. Don't try both partially.*

*Three — make the AI inventory real and current.* Tooling is fine; what matters is that someone owns keeping it accurate. Quarterly review minimum.

*Four — run one incident-response tabletop exercise.* Pick a realistic scenario. Walk through it. *Find the gaps. Close them.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Try to list every AI system in production in your org from memory.* Then compare against what your IT, marketing, customer service, and procurement teams have actually deployed. *The gap between those two lists is your governance debt.*
