Chapter six. *The leadership commitment dimension.* The least technical and most decisive. *Sponsorship, funding, ROI patience.*

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 components]

Three components of leadership commitment readiness.

*Component one — executive sponsorship.* Is there a named C-suite owner of AI as a portfolio? Not just a CIO who got assigned to AI. *A leader who actively champions it in board meetings, allocates capital, kills failing initiatives.*

*Component two — funded multi-year roadmap.* AI doesn't pay back in a single budget cycle. *Is there a three-year capital plan with the patience to absorb year-one investment for year-two-and-three returns?*

*Component three — ROI patience.* The willingness to keep funding AI initiatives that haven't yet shown returns. *Or — equally important — to kill them when the data says they should die. Stage-gate discipline.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 failure modes]

Three failure modes I see consistently.

*Failure mode one — the sponsor changes too often.* CIO who championed AI leaves. New CIO has different priorities. Initiatives stall. *AI portfolio resets every eighteen months.*

*Failure mode two — funded once, then defunded after year one.* Year-one results don't match the hype. Finance pulls funding. *Year-two and year-three returns are never realised.*

*Failure mode three — no stage-gate discipline.* Every initiative gets perpetual funding. *Nothing gets killed. Portfolio gets bigger and bigger and lower and lower quality.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the C-suite stress test]

The C-suite stress test. *Three questions for your CEO.*

*Question one — name three AI initiatives the organisation is investing in this year and the expected business outcome of each.* If your CEO can't, the sponsorship layer is too thin.

*Question two — what's the multi-year AI capital plan?* If the answer is "we'll figure it out year by year," that's red.

*Question three — what AI initiative did we kill in the last twelve months and why?* If the answer is "nothing," the stage-gate discipline isn't working. *Every healthy portfolio kills some bets.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:15]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score leadership commitment honestly. *Three questions.*

*Question one — named C-suite owner of AI portfolio with budget authority?* Yes is green. Sort of is amber. No is red.

*Question two — multi-year capital plan funded?* Yes is green. Annual cycle is amber. *Reactive funding is red.*

*Question three — at least one AI initiative killed at a stage-gate in the last year?* Yes is green. Considered but not done is amber. No is red.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Have a fifteen-minute conversation with your CEO or board chair.* Ask the three stress-test questions. *Their answers tell you exactly where the leadership commitment dimension scores.* That answer is the most important diagnostic in this entire course.
