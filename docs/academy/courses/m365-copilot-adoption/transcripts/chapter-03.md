The cost stack. *Microsoft three sixty-five Copilot enterprise — thirty dollars per user per month.* That's incremental to the M three sixty-five license cost.

What's included. *Copilot in Word, Excel, PowerPoint, Outlook, Teams.* Microsoft Graph integration with permission inheritance. *Tenant-isolated processing.* Microsoft Adoption Score + Viva Insights basic.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · who gets it]

The who-gets-it decision. *Three patterns.*

*Pattern one — universal.* Everyone with an M three sixty-five license gets Copilot. *Simpler. More expensive.* For a five-thousand-person org, one-point-eight million annually.

*Pattern two — manager and knowledge-worker tier.* Targeted to roles that produce documents, decks, analyses, meetings. *Typically eight hundred to fifteen hundred users in a five-thousand-person org.* Half the cost. Most of the value.

*Pattern three — opt-in pilot first.* Start with two to three hundred users across functions. *Measure adoption and value over a quarter.* Then expand based on data.

Most companies should pattern-three first. *Then go to pattern two.* Few should default to pattern one.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:20]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · business case]

The business case. *Active-user productivity gain × active-user count × time saved per user × value per hour — minus licensing cost.*

Worked example. *Thousand active users × thirty percent time savings × one routine document per day × twenty minutes saved × hundred dollars per hour value.* Approximately ten million dollars in productivity annually.

Licensing cost for those thousand active users — *three hundred sixty thousand annually.* Net — *nine-point-six million.* Real economics. *But only if your active-user fraction is high.*

If only thirty percent of the thousand licensees become active — *productivity is three million, license cost is unchanged at three sixty thousand, net is two-point-six million.* Still positive. But materially less impressive.

The narrative for the CFO. *Active-user fraction is the critical variable.* Active-user fraction is a function of change management.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:25]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · pilot pattern]

The pilot pattern. *Pilot in week one. Scale at month three. Embed by month six.* Stage-gate methodology from chapter five of the manager course applies.

*Pilot gate criteria — fifty percent of pilot users become active by end of quarter one.* Scale gate — *active-user fraction maintained at five times pilot scale.* Embed gate — *active-user fraction holds at the end of month six with no additional change-management effort.*

If any gate fails — *don't expand. Diagnose. Either active-user fraction is the gap, or the use cases are wrong, or the change management isn't working.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Build the business case for your organisation.* Use realistic active-user fraction — thirty to forty percent if you've never done it, fifty to sixty if you have strong change management. *Calculate net value across the next twelve months.* Use that number as your decision-bar for the licensing strategy.
