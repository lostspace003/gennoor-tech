> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · EU tiers]

The EU AI Act tiers. *Four levels.*

*Prohibited.* Social scoring of natural persons. Real-time biometric identification in public spaces. Manipulation of vulnerable people. *These are banned. Article Ninety-Nine fines for prohibited AI go up to seven percent of global turnover.*

*High-risk.* Annex Three enumerates the categories. Employment and hiring AI. Education AI affecting access. Critical infrastructure. Law enforcement. Biometric identification. *Credit scoring and insurance — high-risk.* Migration and border control. Justice and democratic processes.

*Limited risk.* Chatbots interacting with humans must disclose AI nature. *Article Fifty.* This is most customer-facing AI in business.

*Minimal risk.* Everything else. *Most enterprise AI productivity tools — drafting, summarising — fall here.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · NIST]

The NIST approach. *Context-based.* The NIST AI RMF doesn't enumerate categories. *It assesses risk based on context.* Same model in different deployments has different risk profiles.

The combined approach most boards use. *EU tiering as the categorisation framework. NIST context-based assessment within each tier.* That is — *use EU tiering to label the system as high-risk or limited. Then use NIST functions to govern it.* The two frameworks compose.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · risk taxonomy]

The risk taxonomy. *Five categories every AI deployment carries some quantity of.*

*Bias and fairness.* The Bloomberg resume study from March twenty twenty-four is the cautionary tale — *reproducible bias in identical-resume ranking.* Especially material in employment, credit, insurance.

*Hallucination and accuracy.* Stanford's seventeen to thirty-four percent number from chapter one of the legal course. *Material wherever the AI produces statements that are taken as factual.*

*Security and privacy.* Prompt injection. Data leakage. Model theft. *Material wherever sensitive data touches the AI.*

*Intellectual property.* Training-data IP issues. Output-IP issues. *Material wherever AI generates content used commercially.*

*Operational and concentration.* Vendor dependency. Model deprecation. Cost shock. *Material across the board.*
