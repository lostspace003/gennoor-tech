The principle.

*AI risk doesn't fit cleanly into the existing risk universe.* Most audit committees still treat it as an operational risk subcategory — when it's actually four risk types at once.

The discipline. *Locate AI risk in all the buckets it touches, not just one.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:45]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 buckets AI touches]

Four risk buckets AI touches simultaneously.

*Bucket one — operational.* Model outage. Latency. Concurrency failure. *Where most committees place AI today. Necessary, not sufficient.*

*Bucket two — compliance.* EU AI Act. GDPR. DPDPA. HIPAA. SAMA. RBI. *Each regulator looks at AI differently. Compliance risk is now multi-jurisdictional by default.*

*Bucket three — strategic.* Building AI on the wrong platform. Locking into vendor that gets sunset. *Strategic risk that takes 18-24 months to surface.*

*Bucket four — reputational.* Hallucinated customer-facing answer. Bias incident. AI-generated material with errors. *Cross-domain cases: Mata v. Avianca, Air Canada Moffatt.*

Most committees see only operational. *The other three are where the surprises come from.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 don't-fit risks]

Three risks AI introduces that don't map cleanly to legacy buckets.

*Don't-fit one — model behaviour drift.* Model worked yesterday. Worked last quarter. Doesn't work this week because vendor updated. *No traditional control framework catches this.*

*Don't-fit two — bias risk.* Same model, different fairness profiles for different demographic groups. Not operational, not compliance — until it becomes a discrimination lawsuit.

*Don't-fit three — cognitive offloading at decision boundaries.* Decisions that used to require human judgement now happen without it. *The risk lives in process design, not control failure.*

These three need new framing. *NIST AI RMF tries. We'll use it in chapter 3.*
