> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the split problem]

The split problem.

Modern boards have multiple committees. *Audit committee oversees risk + controls + financial reporting integrity.* *Risk committee oversees enterprise risk.* *Technology committee oversees technology strategy.* *ESG committee oversees ethics and societal impact.*

AI risk touches all four. *Without explicit coordination, it falls between.*

Common pattern. *Each committee assumes another is covering AI deeply. None actually is.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4-committee coordination]

The four-committee coordination model.

*Audit committee — controls + assurance.* Are AI controls operating? Is audit testing them?

*Risk committee — risk universe + appetite.* Where does AI sit in enterprise risk? Are we within appetite?

*Technology committee — strategy + investment.* What AI is on the roadmap? Build/buy/compose? Vendor portfolio?

*ESG committee — ethics + impact.* Bias. Fairness. Societal impact. Customer-facing AI norms.

Each committee owns its slice. *Audit ensures no gap.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 coordination patterns]

Three coordination patterns that work.

*Pattern one — annual joint AI session.* All four committees meet together once a year. Walk the AI portfolio. Confirm coverage. Surface gaps.

*Pattern two — cross-committee charter language.* Each committee charter mentions AI in its scope. *Specific clauses, not generic.* Ensures committees know what they own.

*Pattern three — escalation taxonomy.* Defined which incidents go to which committee. *AI bias incident → ESG + Audit. Model drift incident → Risk + Technology. Vendor breach → Audit + Technology.*
