The discipline applied honestly.

*Inherent risk.* Risk before any controls. What the AI system could do at its worst.

*Residual risk.* Risk after current controls operating as designed.

The gap between the two is your control effectiveness. *Easy to write. Hard to do honestly.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:45]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 inherent dimensions]

Four inherent risk dimensions for AI.

*Dimension one — output sensitivity.* What's the worst output the model could produce? *Wrong medical advice. Biased loan decision. Hallucinated regulator submission. Air Canada Moffatt cross-domain.*

*Dimension two — automation depth.* Is the AI input, assisted, or deciding? *Inherent risk scales with automation depth.*

*Dimension three — population affected.* One internal user? Ten thousand external customers? *Inherent risk scales with population reach.*

*Dimension four — recovery difficulty.* If the bad output happens, how hard to recover? *Public correction? Lawsuit? Regulatory action? Inherent risk scales with recovery cost.*

Rate each dimension high/medium/low. *That gives you inherent risk vector.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · controls-assumed anti-pattern]

The "controls assumed" anti-pattern.

The pattern. *Inherent risk high. Stated controls in place. Residual rated low.*

Audit committee accepts. *Six months later, control failed during incident. Residual was not actually low — it was nominal.*

The fix. *Test controls before accepting residual ratings.* For high-inherent items, sample tests. Walk-throughs. Live demonstration. Not "policy exists."

When you can't test. *Residual should be amber until tested. Not green by assumption.*
