Chapter three. *AI for interviews* — and the surprising finding from 2022 research that finally clarifies what AI should actually do here. Hint — it's not generating clever chat questions.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:18]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · sackett]

The research that matters. *Sackett, Zhang, Berry, and Lievens* published in the Journal of Applied Psychology in 2022 — a modern update to the Schmidt and Hunter meta-analyses from 1998. They corrected a long-standing statistical issue called range restriction overcorrection. *The result rewrote the textbooks.*

*Structured interviews emerged as the strongest single predictor of job performance* — mean operational validity of approximately point four two. *Higher than cognitive-ability tests.* Higher than personality tests. Higher than every other selection method that's been studied. That validity comes from one thing — *the structure*, not the questions.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · what this means for ai]

What this means for AI's role. *AI's value in interviewing isn't generating questions.* It's *enforcing structured-interview discipline* — the discipline most interviewers abandon under deadline pressure. *Same questions, same order, same rubric applied to every candidate for the same role.* That's the validity driver.

The three concrete AI use cases that earn their cost. *One — generate role-aligned scorecards from the job description.* Translate "we need a senior data engineer" into a structured rubric covering domain knowledge, problem-solving, collaboration, and judgment. *Two — coach interviewer consistency.* AI listens to (or reads) the interview transcript and flags drift from the rubric. *Three — synthesise across interviewers.* Take three interviewers' scorecards for the same candidate and surface the disagreements that need a calibration conversation.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:25]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · hirevue]

What NOT to do — the *HireVue cautionary tale*. HireVue ran a video-interview product that analysed candidates' facial expressions and used them as a predictive signal. *In January 2021 they discontinued the facial-analysis component* after FTC complaints and academic criticism. *The vendor's own concession* — facial cues did not add predictive validity over language analysis. *They were measuring noise.*

The lesson generalises. *Any AI signal that isn't directly tied to job-relevant competencies — discontinue it.* Tone of voice. Facial micro-expressions. Eye contact patterns. Background environment. These are all easy for AI to extract and impossible to validate as predictive. *They're the modern equivalent of phrenology.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:25]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · illinois]

The compliance overlay. *Illinois Artificial Intelligence Video Interview Act, 820 ILCS 42*, in effect since January 2020. If you analyse video interviews with AI in Illinois, you must — *notify candidates before the interview*, *explain how the AI works and what it evaluates*, *obtain explicit consent*, *not share videos beyond evaluators*, and *delete videos within thirty days of a candidate request including backups*.

The principle generalises beyond Illinois. *Transparency about what AI is analysing is the floor.* Hiding it makes the lawsuit easier when it comes.

The interactive on screen. *Pick a role*. *Pick four competency areas*. The tool generates a *structured-interview scorecard* — five questions per competency, a four-point rubric, and a calibration prompt. Copy it. *Use it in your next loop.* That's the workflow.

> [S6 ▸ reveal 2 · the discipline]

The pattern. *The rubric IS the validity.* If three interviewers use the same rubric, score independently, then calibrate — you've just achieved the Sackett point four two on your own funnel. *Without the rubric, you've got an unstructured conversation with a charismatic candidate winning every time.*

> [S7 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:10]

> [S7 ▸ reveal 1 · monday move]

The Monday move. *Pick your next open interview loop.* Use the AI to generate a structured rubric. Send it to all three interviewers *before* the loop. *Score independently. Calibrate after.* You'll know within one loop whether you ship more confident decisions. *Most teams do.*
