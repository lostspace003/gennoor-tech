Chapter four. Candidate experience copilots — chatbots, FAQ bots, status-update agents. *One design decision separates copilots that lift candidate NPS from copilots that destroy candidate trust.* This chapter is that decision.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:25]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the data]

The data first. *The 2024 Talent Board CandE benchmark.* Candidate resentment — defined as "very negative experience" — hit an *all-time high* in 2024. *Twenty-nine percent of North American candidates reported not hearing back at all one to two months after applying.* Forty-eight percent of rejected candidates were unhappy with the feedback they received; only thirty-three percent rated it positively. *Tech and Finance/Insurance — twenty-five percent resentment rate versus the fourteen percent average.*

That's the baseline candidate experience teams are now competing against. *Candidates expect responsiveness. They're getting silence.* A well-designed AI copilot can close that gap. *A poorly-designed one widens it.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:25]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the decision]

The one design decision. *Transparency.* Does the candidate know they're talking to AI, or do they think they're talking to a recruiter? *This is not a UX preference. It's the legal floor in Illinois under the AI Video Interview Act, and it's the EU AI Act requirement for AI systems interacting with humans.*

The research is consistent — *candidates who know they're talking to AI rate the experience higher when the AI is good, and they trust the company more when it's bad.* Candidates who *don't* know they're talking to AI and figure it out — and they always figure it out — *report dramatically lower trust in the company*. Hidden AI is the design choice that turns a productivity win into a brand crisis.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:25]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · what to disclose]

What disclosure actually looks like. *Three things on the opening message.* One — *this is an AI assistant.* Two — *here's what I can help with*. Three — *here's how to reach a human if you need one*. That's it. *Three sentences.*

The mistake teams make — burying the AI disclosure in the privacy policy. *That doesn't count.* The candidate has to know before the first question they ask. The Illinois AIVIA standard for video interviews is the right floor everywhere — *explicit consent before AI is used*.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:20]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · escalation]

The escalation path. *Eighty percent of candidate FAQs the AI can handle alone* — status, process, timing, role details. *Twenty percent need a human.* Salary negotiations. Visa-status discussions. Reference-check followups. Disability-accommodation requests. *Build the escalation path before you ship.*

The pattern that works — the AI offers escalation proactively when it detects high-stakes topics, *not* when the candidate has to ask three times. *A copilot that makes the candidate fight to reach a human is a copilot that's already failed.*

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:15]

The interactive on screen. *Pick a copilot persona.* Choose its FAQ scope. Choose its escalation rule. Choose its transparency level. *The tool generates a one-page spec you can hand to your vendor.* Most copilot-vendor conversations stall because the buyer hasn't decided these four things in advance. *Decide them now.*

The pattern. Transparency + clear scope + proactive escalation + measured response time. *Get those four right and your CandE score lifts. Get any one wrong and you're in the twenty-five percent resentment club.*
