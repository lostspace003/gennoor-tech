> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Chapter six. Workforce planning AI — *scenario planning at the team and org level*. Deloitte and McKinsey both published in twenty twenty-four arguing the traditional annual workforce plan is broken in an AI-disrupted labour market. *They're right.* This chapter is what the new shape looks like, and one specific failure mode to watch for.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:25]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · what works]

What works. *Three concrete use cases.*

*One — scenario planning across attrition, hiring, and skill gaps.* AI runs the model — what if attrition spikes ten percent in engineering, what does hiring need to look like, what skills are we short. *That's scenario planning. That's good.*

*Two — skills-gap detection from internal data.* Compare what your team can do today against what the roadmap requires next year. AI surfaces the gap. *Then a human decides whether to hire, train, or de-scope.*

*Three — pipeline forecasting.* Given current funnel velocity and historical conversion rates, when does the team hit full headcount? *Useful for budget conversations. Not useful as a commitment.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:25]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · failure mode]

The failure mode to watch for. *Models trained on past workforce patterns reproduce those patterns* — including the bias, the shortage points, and the assumptions that no longer hold.

If your historical hiring data over-indexes on candidates from three universities, *the workforce planning AI predicts you'll keep needing those candidates*. If your past attrition pattern reflected an open labour market in a specific city, *the AI predicts attrition that no longer matches reality*. If your skills taxonomy is two years stale, *the AI extrapolates skills that are no longer relevant*. **Models don't predict — they project the past into the future.**

The mitigation isn't to abandon the AI. *It's to use the AI for what it's good at* — *running many scenarios fast* — *and humans for what they're good at* — *judging which scenario is plausible*. Scenario planning, not prediction.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:35]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · picker]

The interactive on screen. Three labour-market scenarios. *For each one, pick whether AI workforce planning predicts it well, or fails badly.* The pattern that emerges — *AI works for plausible variations on history, fails for genuine discontinuities.*

> [pause for hands-on · ≈ 10 seconds]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 2 · pattern]

The pattern. *Use AI for "what if this trend continues" — fine. Use AI for "what if this trend reverses" — only as one of multiple scenarios.* Don't anchor on a single AI forecast for any genuinely uncertain future state.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:25]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · vendor note]

A vendor-landscape note for context. *Visier, Beamery, and Eightfold* are the three vendors most TA teams will encounter for workforce-planning AI. *Each is moving toward agentic-scenario workflows in 2025-2026.* The buying question isn't "does the vendor do scenario planning" — they all do. *The question is whether the vendor's data model fits your actual workforce taxonomy* — your roles, your skills, your levels. *The implementation work is the data work.*

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:00]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · monday move]

The Monday move. *Take your current twenty twenty-six hiring plan.* Ask the workforce-planning AI you have — or just the AI you have access to — to run three scenarios: *historical pattern continues, twenty percent attrition spike, market freezes.* Get three numbers, not one. *That's the discipline you'll keep when the labour market actually surprises you.*
