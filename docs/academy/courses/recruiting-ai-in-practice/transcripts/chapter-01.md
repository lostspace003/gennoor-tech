## Transcript

> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Welcome to Recruiting AI in Practice. Eight chapters across the full hiring funnel — sourcing through onboarding — with the bias and compliance posture that keeps you out of trouble. Chapter one is the front of the funnel. *Sourcing has changed more in the last twelve months than in the previous five years.* This chapter is what changed, what works in 2026, and where it can break.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:30]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the shift]

The shift in one sentence. *The TA stack moved from recruiter-driven, to agent-augmented, to agent-operated.* The analyst firm Aptitude Research's 2025 year-in-review names the three biggest moves. *hireEZ launched EZ Agent* — autonomous sourcing, engagement, and pipeline refresh. *Findem acquired Getro and launched Intelligent Job Post* — agents that source, engage, and qualify candidates without a recruiter in the loop. *Eightfold repositioned from talent intelligence to agentic intelligence.* The vendors are all moving the same direction. You're not getting back to keyword Boolean.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · linkedin numbers]

The numbers that matter. *LinkedIn launched Hiring Assistant* — their first AI agent — at the end of September 2025. *Customers save more than four hours per user per role.* Recruiters see *sixty-six percent higher InMail acceptance rates* with Hiring Assistant versus traditional sourcing. *AI-Assisted Messages drive fifty-five percent higher InMail acceptance.* Users find good candidates while reviewing *less than half* the profiles they used to.

That's the value proposition in real numbers, from LinkedIn's own customer reporting. The February 2026 quarterly update added Teams collaboration, AI Follow-Ups, AI Applicant Targeting, and Verified Applicant Spotlight. *The product is improving every quarter.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · what makes intent based work]

What makes intent-based sourcing actually work — versus the keyword search you grew up with. *You stop describing the candidate and start describing the role outcome.* Instead of *"Python, Django, five years, San Francisco"* you tell the agent *"we need someone who can scale our subscription billing system from ten thousand to a hundred thousand customers without rewriting it."* The agent translates that into search planning, candidate evaluation, and outreach. *It's a brief, not a query.*

> [S4 ▸ reveal 2 · what changes for the recruiter]

What this changes for the recruiter. You spend less time *finding* candidates and more time *qualifying* the ones the agent surfaced. *That's the actual labour shift.* Sourcing recruiters who used to spend sixty percent of their week on Boolean searches now spend that time on the conversations that need a human. *That's where the four-hours-per-role saving comes from.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:15]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the trap]

The trap. *AI-generated outreach at scale destroys your employer brand.* LinkedIn capped Open InMail sends to *under one hundred per month* in late 2025 — down from a practical limit of around eight hundred. *An eighty-seven percent overnight drop in outbound capacity.* The platform is throttling AI-spam at the network layer because candidate response rates collapsed when everyone used the same generative-outreach playbook.

The lesson is operational. *More candidates is no longer the goal. Better candidates is.* Personalisation that survives reading must be specific to that candidate's actual work — their last shipped project, a talk they gave, a paper they wrote. Generative outreach that fills in the blanks of a template *no longer works*. Recruiters who haven't internalised this are sending hundreds of messages and getting nothing back.

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:25]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · the eu gotcha]

The European gotcha — important for any team hiring in or from the EU. *EU candidate outreach falls under GDPR Article 6, paragraph 1, sub-paragraph f — legitimate interest.* The October 2024 Court of Justice ruling plus the new EDPB guidelines *raised the documentation bar.* You need a written Legitimate Interest Assessment — the three-part test of purpose, necessity, and balancing. You need to honour opt-outs within twenty-four to forty-eight hours.

For recruiters in the GCC or India sourcing into Europe — *you inherit the EU rules the moment your message lands in an EU resident's inbox.* This is not optional. The penalties under GDPR scale to four percent of global turnover. *Build the LIA into your sourcing playbook.*

> [S7 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:25]

> [S7 ▸ reveal 1 · the comparator]

The interactive on screen. Five sourcing scenarios. For each one, pick — *keyword Boolean*, *AI-assisted message*, *intent-based agent*, or *do not contact*. The "do not contact" answers are the GDPR traps. *Two scenarios are deliberately tricky.*

> [pause for hands-on · ≈ 10 seconds]

> [S7 ▸ reveal 2 · the pattern]

The pattern across the right answers — *match the tool to the candidate context, not to the recruiter's habit*. Specific senior hire with a long passive history → intent-based agent with documented LIA. High-volume req with a clear must-have → AI-assisted message. Single backfill in a saturated market → still might be keyword Boolean and a phone call. *The tools coexist. Pick by context.*

> [S8 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 6:15]

> [S8 ▸ reveal 1 · monday move]

The Monday move. *Pick one open req. Brief it as a one-paragraph outcome statement instead of a job description.* Feed it to LinkedIn Hiring Assistant or your equivalent. See what the agent surfaces. *Compare it to your existing Boolean shortlist for the same role.* If the agent's list looks different in a useful way, you've just learned the new sourcing skill. *Five minutes to set up. Tomorrow afternoon to evaluate.*

Next chapter — the screening layer. *Where the bias problem actually lives, and the four mitigations every TA team should adopt this quarter.*
