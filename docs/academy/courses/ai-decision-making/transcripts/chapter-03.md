> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 override signals]

Three signals your judgement is correct and the model's isn't.

*Signal one — context the model can't see.* Internal politics. Team dynamics. Specific customer history. *If you're holding context the model wasn't trained on, your judgement is more informed.*

*Signal two — recency.* The model's training cutoff is six to twelve months ago. *If the question is about what changed last quarter, your knowledge is fresher.*

*Signal three — regional specifics.* Regulations, market conditions, cultural norms in your specific market. *Especially relevant in GCC, India, Africa, SEA where the model's training data underrepresents the region.*

When you hold any of these three. *Override is the right call.* And do it without apology.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the deference trap]

The deference trap.

The pattern. *"The model said X. We followed. It didn't work. We're confused why."*

The cause. *You held information the model didn't have. You knew it. You overrode internally — then deferred to the model in writing.*

This happens when leaders feel they need to justify a decision externally and the model's framing is convenient cover. *"The model recommends X" becomes the rationale, even when your judgement was Y.*

The cost. *You lose the lesson when X doesn't work.* Was X wrong because the model was wrong, or because you abandoned your own reasoning? *You won't know.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:45]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the override discipline]

The override discipline.

*When you override.* Write a one-line note: what the model said, what you decided, why your context overrode it.

That note serves three purposes. *Audit trail for later — was the override good or bad? Pattern detection — when do you keep overriding? Team teaching — others learn when override is correct.*

Without the note. *Overrides become invisible.* You can't learn from your own pattern.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:45]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score override discipline. *Two questions.*

*Question one — when you override AI, do you document why?* Yes consistently green · sometimes amber · *never red — no learning loop*.

*Question two — looking at your last 10 overrides, what fraction turned out to be correct calls?* >70% green · 50-70% amber · *<50% red — your overrides aren't well-calibrated*.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pull your last 10 AI-augmented decisions.* For ones where you overrode — were you right? *That calibration is what tells you when to trust your override muscle.*
