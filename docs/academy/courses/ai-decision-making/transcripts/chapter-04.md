> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the principle]

The principle.

You can't verify every AI-augmented decision. *That would defeat the purpose of using AI in the first place.*

You also can't verify none of them. *That's how you end up in the deference trap.*

The 80/20 rule. *Verify the 20% of decisions where the cost of being wrong is 80% of the total decision-stakes.* That's where verification energy belongs.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · which 20% to verify]

Which 20% you actually verify.

*Reversibility filter.* If the decision is hard to reverse, verify it. If easy to reverse, skip detailed verification.

*Stakes filter.* High-stakes decisions — money, people, legal — verify. Low-stakes — daily prioritization, routine choices — skip.

*Context filter.* Decisions involving context the model can't see — internal dynamics, regional specifics — verify. Decisions in domains the model knows well — usually skip.

If two of three filters say verify. *Verify carefully.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 100% verification cases]

Three cases that need 100% verification, not 20%.

*Case one — anything published externally as fact.* Customer-facing claims. Regulator submissions. Marketing copy that asserts numbers. *Mata v. Avianca cross-domain. Air Canada Moffatt cross-domain.*

*Case two — anything affecting individual rights.* Hiring decisions. Compensation. Termination. Customer-facing denials. *Bias, error, and consequence make this a line.*

*Case three — anything triggering legal disclosure.* Securities-relevant. Material risk disclosure. Compliance reporting. *The cost of a wrong-but-defensible answer is much smaller than the cost of an undisclosed material fact.*

For these, *verify every claim. Cite every source. Trace every number.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

How to score verification discipline. *Two questions.*

*Question one — for the 100%-verify categories, do you have a documented verification protocol?* Yes written down green · informal amber · *no red — incident is statistically inevitable*.

*Question two — for the 20% you choose to verify, is the choice deliberate or random?* Deliberate green · "we check what looks suspicious" amber · *random red — you're verifying noise, missing signal*.
