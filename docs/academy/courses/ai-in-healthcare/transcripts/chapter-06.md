What payers deploy AI for. *Prior authorisation review — AI screens prior-auth requests against coverage criteria.* Claims processing — AI flags claims for human review. *Fraud detection. Utilization management.*

The value proposition. *Faster decisions. Lower administrative cost. Consistent rule application.* Payers report material operational savings from AI-augmented utilization management.

The controversy. *Multiple investigations through twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-five.* ProPublica investigation into a large payer's AI-driven prior-authorisation denials. *ABC News investigation into similar patterns.* Class-action litigation alleging AI was used to systematically deny medically-necessary care.

What went wrong in the documented cases. *AI scoring or filtering of prior-auth requests with insufficient physician review on denials.* When the AI denial happened — *patients and providers had limited path to appeal.* Some cases — appeals also went through automated review.

The legal exposure. *Denial of medically-necessary care is actionable.* Several large payers settled for material sums through twenty twenty-four. *State regulators in California, New York, Texas have opened formal investigations into payer AI through twenty twenty-five.*

The proposed federal rules. *CMS proposed rules on prior-auth AI transparency through twenty twenty-five.* Requiring disclosure of AI use in coverage decisions. *Requiring physician review of denials within specific timeframes.* These are the early signs of mandatory regulation, not just litigation.

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the line]

The line between efficiency and harm. *Where it sits — based on the case law and regulator positions emerging.*

*Efficiency side.* AI-augmented document review. AI flagging straightforward approvals for fast-tracking. *AI surfacing complete versus incomplete prior-auth submissions.* All these are operationally sensible without harming patients.

*Harm side.* AI-driven denials without timely human review. *AI scoring that systematically denies subgroups of patients.* Automated appeal denial. *Denial of care without clear physician-level engagement.*

The principle. *Approvals can be AI-augmented.* Denials cannot be AI-only. A licensed clinician — typically a physician at the payer — must be in the loop on denials of medically-necessary care.
