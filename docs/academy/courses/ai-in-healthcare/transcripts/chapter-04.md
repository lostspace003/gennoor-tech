Chapter four. *Radiology and diagnostic AI.* The most-deployed FDA-cleared AI category. *Over one thousand devices on the FDA list by mid twenty twenty-five — eighty percent or so are imaging.* This chapter is what's working, what's not, and what radiologist-in-the-loop actually means.

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · what's deployed]

What's deployed in twenty twenty-six. *Aidoc — broad-spectrum radiology AI, deployed across hundreds of US and EU hospitals.* Annalise AI — chest x-ray and radiology AI. *Heuron, Viz dot AI, RapidAI, Aidoc — competing platforms for time-critical findings.*

What they do. *AI reads imaging studies for time-sensitive findings* — stroke, pulmonary embolism, intracranial haemorrhage, aortic dissection. *The AI flags suspected positives for radiologist review.* Speed-to-diagnosis can be material — minutes saved on stroke detection translate to brain tissue preserved.

The FDA SaMD pathway. *Software as a Medical Device — premarket approval framework refined since twenty seventeen.* The FDA reviews clinical evidence — *sensitivity, specificity, comparison to standard of care.*

The published list — *over one thousand AI-enabled medical devices by mid twenty twenty-five.* Updated regularly. *Hospital procurement teams should check the list — if the AI vendor isn't on it for the clinical use case, ask why.* For some applications, the FDA path is being navigated. For others, the use case isn't intended for FDA approval. *Either is fine — but the question must be asked.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:10]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the radiologist-in-loop]

Radiologist-in-the-loop. *The deployment pattern that meets the responsibility line.* AI flags or surfaces findings. *Radiologist reads the imaging and confirms or refutes.* The radiologist's reading is the diagnosis. Not the AI's.

False positives. *AI errs on the side of flagging — sensitivity higher than specificity.* That's by design — better to over-flag a possible stroke than to miss one. *But the radiologist sees more flags than true positives.* The discipline — radiologist time spent on triage and review is the cost. *Net time saved still positive on well-tuned platforms. Net negative on poorly-tuned ones.*

False negatives. *AI misses some findings the radiologist would catch.* This is the safety risk. *Mitigation — AI is supplementary, not replacement. Radiologist reads independently. AI provides a second look, not a replacement look.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:20]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · operational deployment]

Operational deployment. *Three patterns by twenty twenty-six.*

*Pattern one — workflow triage.* AI sorts the worklist by urgency. Stroke and PE moved to top. Routine to bottom. *Radiologist reads in priority order.*

*Pattern two — second read.* Radiologist reads independently. AI provides a second-look comparison. *Discrepancies trigger detailed review.* Higher-confidence final reading.

*Pattern three — pre-screen.* AI screens normal-presumed scans. Radiologist confirms. *Higher throughput on routine reads.*

All three patterns require the radiologist as the diagnosing professional. *Liability remains with the radiologist.*
