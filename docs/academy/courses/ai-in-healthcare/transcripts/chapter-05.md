> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · what's deployed]

What's deployed in twenty twenty-six. *Patient chatbots for appointment scheduling, prescription refills, billing questions.* Symptom-checker AI integrated with patient portals. *Discharge instruction AI — translating physician instructions into patient-friendly language.* Post-discharge follow-up bots.

What works well. *Routine transactional interactions.* Scheduling. Status updates. *Frequently-asked-questions automation.* Time savings for nurses and patient-services staff are material.

What doesn't work — *anything that veers into medical advice without physician review.* Symptom-checker AI in particular has been the subject of multiple controversies in twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-four.

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · Air Canada analog]

The Air Canada Moffatt cross-domain. *Anything the AI says on the hospital's behalf becomes the hospital's statement.*

Translate to patient-facing AI. *If a chatbot says "your insurance will cover this procedure" and it doesn't — the hospital is on the hook.* If a chatbot says "you don't need to see a doctor for that symptom" and the patient is having an MI — that's medical malpractice exposure.

The mitigation. *Retrieval-grounded on hospital-approved information.* Quote-or-cut on coverage and clinical statements. *Escalation triggers — anything that touches diagnosis, treatment, coverage commitments — escalate to human.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:10]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · EU + UK + India]

The regulatory frame. *EU AI Act Article Fifty transparency — patient must be told when interacting with AI.* Effective August second twenty twenty-six.

*HIPAA — patient data handling.* In the US, patient-facing AI must respect minimum-necessary and de-identification.

*UK FCA Consumer Duty doesn't directly apply* — but UK NHS guidance on AI in patient interactions is evolving.

*India DPDPA twenty twenty-three* — health data is sensitive. Consent for AI use required.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · patient consent]

Patient consent for AI use. *The emerging best practice — explicit consent at patient intake for AI to be used in their care.*

The script. *"Some of your interactions with our hospital may involve AI tools that assist staff and clinicians. You may opt out at any time. AI does not replace your clinician's judgement."*

Three sentences. *Three commitments — disclosure, opt-out, clinical primacy.* This satisfies EU AI Act Article Fifty. *Aligns with US HIPAA principles.* And is increasingly the bar in clinical-ethics literature.

The discipline. *Honour the opt-out.* If a patient declines, the hospital must have a non-AI-assisted workflow available. *Don't ask for consent if you can't honour the no.*
