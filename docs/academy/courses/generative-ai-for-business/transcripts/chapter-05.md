Chapter five. *Running pilots that scale.* Most AI pilots don't scale. *Gartner — under thirty percent of AI projects deliver measurable business value.* This chapter is how to be in the thirty percent.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · stage-gate]

The methodology. *Stage-gate.* Three gates. Each gate has a kill-or-fund decision.

*Gate one — pilot gate.* Pilot in one team for one quarter. *Measure against the use case you framed in chapter two.* Did it deliver the time saving or quality lift you projected? If yes — fund to scale. If no — kill cleanly.

*Gate two — scale gate.* Scale from one team to ten times the user base. *Does the productivity claim hold at scale?* The active-user definition from chapter three matters here. *Microsoft Work Trend Index — thirty percent time savings for active users.* If only a third of your scaled team is active, your gain is one-tenth, not one-third.

*Gate three — embed gate.* After three months at scale, is the AI tool the default? *Do team members reach for it without being reminded?* If yes — pilot succeeded. If no — the friction wasn't removed. *Investigate where.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · kill discipline]

The kill discipline. *If a pilot doesn't pass its gate — kill it cleanly. Don't extend.*

Why this matters. *Most managers fight to keep their pilots alive even when they don't work.* The optical reasoning — *"I championed this, killing it makes me look bad."* The actual outcome — *spreading attention across multiple stalled pilots, none get the resources to actually move the business.*

The honest version. *Killing a failed pilot is a signal of capital discipline, not failure.* The two pilots that pass your gates get five times the resources they had. *That's how scaling actually happens.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:35]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · measurement]

The measurement framework. *Three things.*

*One — baseline before.* What was the time, cost, or accuracy before AI? Measure this *before* the pilot. *Not retroactively.*

*Two — counterfactual.* What would have happened without the AI? If you'd just hired more people. If you'd given the team better templates. *Compare to that, not to "doing nothing."*

*Three — quality and re-work.* Was the output as good or better than before? Did the team spend additional time verifying or fixing AI mistakes? *The honest measure includes verification time.*

This is not exotic. It's just rigour applied consistently. *The seventy percent of pilots that don't deliver value skip this rigour. The thirty percent that deliver do it.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:35]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the active-user reality]

The active-user reality. *Active users get the productivity gains. Inactive users don't.*

Microsoft Work Trend Index — *thirty percent time savings on routine documents for active Copilot users.* The opposite of active — "I tried it once, it didn't help, I went back to my old way." *That's most people the first month.*

The manager's job. *Make the new way easier than the old way.* Templates. Saved prompts. Shared best practices from active users. Office hours. *Until the AI tool is the default reach.*
