Chapter two. *Framing AI use cases for your team.* The first of the four manager skills. *If you can't frame the use case in one paragraph, you can't pilot it, scale it, or measure it.* This chapter is the framing.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the 4-question framework]

The four-question framework. *Ask these about any candidate AI use case for your team.*

*Question one. What task?* Specific. Not "use AI for marketing." But — "AI drafts the first version of our weekly performance email to the leadership team." Specific task. Specific output.

*Question two. What does it cost today?* Time. Attention. Money. *If the task takes one person two hours per week — that's a manageable scope.* If the task is "write a strategy document once a quarter" — different scope.

*Question three. What data is available?* Does the AI need access to specific documents, customer records, or internal systems? *Without the right data, the AI is just a smart guess machine.* This is the most-skipped question.

*Question four. What's the failure mode?* If the AI gets it wrong, what happens? *Embarrassing internal email — low stakes. Customer-facing commitment — high stakes.* The failure mode shapes the controls.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the 5 categories]

The five categories where gen AI works in twenty twenty-six. *Drafting* — first versions of documents. *Summarising* — long meetings, papers, threads. *Translating* — across languages or registers. *Classifying* — sorting, tagging, routing. *Code generation* — for any team that touches code.

These are the categories where productivity gains are real. *MIT, BCG, Harvard, Goldman through twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-four — fifteen to forty percent gains on knowledge-work tasks.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:25]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · what doesn't]

Where gen AI doesn't fit. *Three categories.* Open-ended research with citations. *Stanford "Hallucinating Law" — seventeen to thirty-four percent hallucination, even with retrieval grounding.* If the work depends on specific facts being correct, AI augments — doesn't replace — a careful human.

*Judgement-heavy decisions.* Hiring choices. Personnel issues. Customer escalations. *AI can inform. Humans decide.*

*Identity-specific facts.* The AI wasn't trained on your specific customers, your specific contracts, your specific personnel. *Don't use it as a substitute for actually knowing.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:20]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the manager pattern]

The manager pattern. *Generate three candidate use cases for your team this quarter.* For each — run the four-question framework. *The ones that pass all four go forward to pilot.* The ones that fail any question — defer or kill.

This is the leverage work. *One hour of your time framing produces months of team output if the use case is right.* Zero output if the use case is wrong.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Pick one task your team does every week.* Run the four-question framework. *If it passes — that's your first pilot. If it doesn't — pick another.* The framing is the deliverable.
