> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 cases]

Three cases your team needs to know about. *Mata versus Avianca — federal court, Southern District of New York, June twenty twenty-three.* Lawyers sanctioned five thousand dollars and ordered to write personal letters to falsely-cited judges after submitting six ChatGPT-fabricated case citations. *Lesson — AI confidently produces specific facts that aren't real. Verify every specific claim.*

*Moffatt versus Air Canada — BC Civil Resolution Tribunal, February twenty twenty-four.* Chatbot promised a bereavement-fare refund the airline didn't actually offer. Customer awarded eight hundred twelve dollars plus interest. *"Air Canada is responsible for all information on its website, including from a chatbot."* The lesson — anything your team's AI says on the company's behalf is the company's statement.

*Bloomberg resume study — March twenty twenty-four.* Identical resumes varied only by names — statistically significant bias in GPT-three-point-five and GPT-four ranking. *The methodology and data are public on Bloomberg's GitHub.* Lesson — AI inherits biases from training data.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:30]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · personal-data rule]

The personal-data rule. *What you put into an AI tool may be retained, processed, and in some cases used to train future models.* This is the most common manager-level mistake.

The rule. *Don't put client-confidential, regulated, or personally-sensitive information into a free consumer AI account.* Use the enterprise version your company has licensed — OpenAI Enterprise, Anthropic Claude for Work, Microsoft Copilot for Microsoft three sixty-five. *Training opt-out and tenant isolation are baseline in enterprise.*

What your team needs from you, the manager. *Clarity on which tool to use for which kind of work.* Personal email about lunch — any tool fine. Customer contract review — enterprise tool only. *Make the rule explicit.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · EU AI Act]

The regulatory framework. *EU AI Act — Regulation twenty twenty-four sixteen-eighty-nine — published August twenty twenty-four. In tranches through August second twenty twenty-six.*

What managers need to know. *Article Fifty transparency — if your team's AI interacts with customers or employees, the AI must say so.* High-risk categories under Annex Three — hiring, credit scoring, biometric identification, education AI affecting access. *If your use case touches any of these, the regulatory bar is higher.*

Article Ninety-Nine fines — *up to fifteen million euros or three percent of global turnover for high-risk failures.* If your company operates in the EU or serves EU customers, this applies.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the 3 risk categories]

The three risk categories your AI use cases fall into.

*Legal risk.* Fabricated citations. Contract misrepresentation. Regulatory non-compliance. *Mitigation — quote-or-cut on AI-generated specific facts.*

*Reputational risk.* Customer-facing AI saying the wrong thing about products, fees, eligibility. *Mitigation — retrieval-grounded on actual policy documents.*

*Customer-data risk.* Sensitive data in free tools. Training-data leakage. *Mitigation — enterprise terms on every tool that touches sensitive data.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *List your team's top three AI use cases.* For each — which of the three risk categories applies? *If any use case has a legal or customer-data risk, draft the mitigation now.* Don't wait for the incident.
