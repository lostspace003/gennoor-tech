The WEF context. *Future of Jobs Report twenty twenty-five.* Eighty-six percent of employers expect to transform their workforce through AI and automation. *Forty percent expect to reduce parts of the workforce by twenty thirty.* These two numbers are not contradictory.

What your team hears. *"My job is about to change. Possibly disappear."* That's the underlying anxiety. *If you don't address it, your team won't adopt.* They'll comply. > [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:10]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the manager's 3 moves]

The manager's three moves.

*Move one — model the behaviour.* You, as the manager, use AI in your own work visibly. *In meetings, in emails, in your own decision-making, you reference what the AI helped you with.* Not as performance — as honest usage.

*Move two — set the expectation.* AI-augmented work is the default, not optional. *Performance conversations include AI fluency.* New hires are evaluated on AI-augmented productivity. Output expectations are calibrated to AI-augmented baselines, not pre-AI baselines.

*Move three — measure the outcome.* From chapter five. Baseline, counterfactual, quality. *Share the measurements with the team.* When the data shows the pilot worked, celebrate. When it didn't, kill cleanly and pick the next one.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the Grove framing]

The Grove framing. *High Output Management.* The manager's job is leverage. *AI is leverage when used correctly.*

What this means practically. *One hour of you framing the right AI use case produces tens of hours of team output.* One hour of you running the pilot stage-gate produces months of productivity.

The opposite — *delegating "go figure out AI" to direct reports.* Each direct report independently figures out a different set of tools, prompts, and workflows. *No leverage. Just fragmentation.* The manager who frames, evaluates, pilots, measures leads. The manager who delegates the leverage decision lags.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the conversations]

The conversations you need to have. Three of them.

*Conversation one — with your team.* "The work of our team is changing. AI is a tool we're going to learn to use well. Here's the pilot. Here's how we'll measure. Here's what I expect from each of you."

*Conversation two — with your boss.* "Here's our AI plan. Here's what we're measuring. Here's what we need from leadership to remove blockers."

*Conversation three — with the AI-resistant team member.* The senior team member who's been doing the work the same way for fifteen years. *Don't shame.* Don't threaten. *Show the data.* "Here's what active users are doing. Here's the productivity delta. I want you to be active by end of next quarter. Here's the support."
