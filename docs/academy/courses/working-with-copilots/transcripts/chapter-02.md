# Chapter 2 — When to use Copilot vs. do it yourself

**Course:** Working with Copilots · **Chapter:** 02 · **Target duration:** ~7 min spoken

## Trainer persona
Emma. Practical, slightly opinionated. Pushes back against Copilot-overuse without being anti-tool.

---

## Transcript

> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Most teams using M365 Copilot in 2026 have the *opposite* problem they had two years ago. Two years ago — nobody used it. Today — people prompt Copilot for tasks they could finish faster by typing the answer themselves. That's the overuse trap, and this chapter is the test that fixes it.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:25]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · time to decent draft]

The test is one sentence. *How long would a decent first draft take you, with no AI help?*

If the answer is more than three minutes — Copilot probably wins. The prompt, the read, the small fix-up, the send — under three minutes total, and you saved time. *That's where Copilot earns its licence cost.*

If the answer is less than three minutes — Copilot probably loses. The time to write a good prompt, plus reading what came back, plus the small correction, plus deciding whether to keep the AI's phrasing or your own — it adds up to more than the original task would have taken. *And you've added a hop where there didn't need to be one.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:25]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the wins]

Where Copilot wins. The Forrester study Microsoft commissioned — caveat that it's Microsoft's own number — pegged the average employee at nine hours per month saved on drafting emails, generating reports, and summarising meetings. Vodafone, with sixty-eight thousand employees on Copilot, reported three to four hours per week per person in trials, with legal department staff specifically saving four hours per week.

The pattern in both numbers — *long-form drafting, multi-source synthesis, and summarisation*. The tasks that took thirty minutes manually now take ten with Copilot doing the heavy structural lift and you doing the polish.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the losses]

Where Copilot loses. Three patterns that always lose.

*One* — prompting for a one-line reply. *"Tell Aisha I'll be there at three."* You can type that in eight seconds. Prompting Copilot takes thirty.

*Two* — prompting for a number you can compute. *"What's twenty-three percent of three thousand four hundred."* You have a calculator. The Copilot answer is right, but the seek time and trust calibration cost more than the math did.

*Three* — prompting for a tone you can't articulate. *"Make this sound more confident."* If you can't tell Copilot what confident means in your context, *you can't tell it's done the right thing.* You'll iterate three times, end up rewriting it yourself, and you've now spent twice the time.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:15]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest about adoption]

A point of honesty from real rollout data. The Australian government ran a whole-of-government Copilot trial with seventy-six hundred staff across sixty-plus agencies — January through June 2024. The Treasury ran a separate 218-person trial from May to August. *Both reported real time savings* — up to an hour on summarising and drafting, fifty to seventy percent perceived speedup on those tasks. Sixty-four percent of managers saw uplift.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the caveat]

*And* — most focus group participants in the whole-of-government trial reported experiencing inaccuracies or hallucinations. The Treasury report explicitly noted Copilot's value *"for more complex work tasks was limited."* Worker attitudes toward AI in the Treasury actually *soured* after the trial.

The takeaway — Copilot wins on the tasks I named, loses on tasks that need genuine reasoning or specialist knowledge. *Knowing which is which is the whole skill.* That's what this chapter trains.

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:25]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · the sorter]

The interactive on screen. Six tasks. For each one, pick — *Copilot* or *do it yourself*. Then check the verdict.

The tasks span the whole range — a short email, a long report draft, a number computation, a tone adjustment, a meeting summary, a creative brainstorm. *Some are obvious. Two of them are deliberately tricky.* You'll get one wrong. That's the point.

> [pause for hands-on · ≈ 10 seconds]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 2 · the lesson]

The pattern across the wins — *complexity, structure, and synthesis*. The pattern across the losses — *atomic, short, or requires judgement you can't externalise*.

If you do this test on every task this week for one week, by Friday you'll have a calibrated gut feel for which tasks belong to Copilot and which belong to your own keyboard. *That's the goal.*

> [S7 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:30]

> [S7 ▸ reveal 1 · monday move]

The Monday move from this chapter. Pick *one* recurring task you do every week that takes more than fifteen minutes. *Hand that one to Copilot for the next four Mondays.* Measure how long the Copilot version takes versus your manual version. By week four, you'll know if it's a keeper.

Pick *one* recurring task that takes less than two minutes. *Promise yourself you won't prompt Copilot for it.* Just type the answer. By Friday, that's an hour you didn't lose to the prompt-and-correct loop.

Next chapter — the four Office workflows everyone uses. Word, Excel, Outlook, Teams. Specific patterns. And the one trap that catches everyone first time — *Copilot grabbing the wrong file because it had three with similar names*.

See you in chapter three.
