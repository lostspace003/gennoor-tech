The principle. *Two use cases. Not seven.* Same discipline as every function in this academy. *Two use cases. Two quarters. One FVA discipline.*

The mistake S&OP teams make. *Trying to overhaul the full forecast stack at once.* Demand sensing, promo uplift, NPI, ensemble layer, LLM narrative — all in one quarter. *Pick two. Ship them with FVA running. Then expand.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

*Quarter one — baseline ML on top one hundred SKUs plus FVA analysis.* Get the foundation right. ML model that's matched to data — LightGBM where appropriate, classical where appropriate, Croston for intermittent. *FVA running every cycle.* Accuracy by SKU class, bias separately, interval coverage tracked.

*Quarter two — one of demand sensing on a single channel OR promotional uplift.* Pick the one with the biggest baseline pain. Demand sensing if you have POS-data quality. Promo uplift if your promo calendar is clean. *FVA on the new layer too.*

*Defer.* NPI methodology. Full multi-channel rollout. LLM-narrative layer in the exec deck. *Year two, after FVA on the basics is solid.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:55]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · trust trip-wires]

Four trust trip-wires. *Hard nos.*

*One — no model that can't be explained.* If your vendor can't tell you the top three features driving the forecast for a specific SKU — *don't ship it to executives.* Explainability is a vendor-RFP requirement.

*Two — no override without FVA recording.* Planner overrides are valuable. *Unrecorded overrides are noise.* Every override gets logged, every quarter reviewed.

*Three — no AI-generated narrative without source check.* The LokadTech rule. Quote or cut. *Every specific claim in S&OP commentary either traces to a number, or it gets cut from the deck.*

*Four — no "set and forget."* FVA is the contract. Quarterly review. *Stop measuring, you've lost the controls.*
