> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the principle]

The principle that runs through this course. *AI products are evaluated like products. Not like models.* Users do not care about model accuracy in the abstract. *They care about whether the product works for their job in their context.*

That changes how AI PMs work. *Job-to-be-done framing. Eval harnesses. Telemetry from day one. Guardrails before users find the edges. Customer feedback as the primary signal — not vanity metrics.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 5 AI PM capabilities]

Five capabilities every AI PM needs.

*Capability one — frame the AI job-to-be-done.* What specific user job does this AI solve. What does success look like. What does failure look like.

*Capability two — design with eval harnesses.* Quantitative evaluation built into the product. Not optional. Not later.

*Capability three — design with telemetry.* Capture user actions, AI outputs, user reactions. Without telemetry you cannot improve.

*Capability four — build guardrails.* What the AI must not say or do. Refusals, fallbacks, escalations.

*Capability five — communicate honestly to customers.* What the AI does well. What it doesn't. *Disclosure builds trust. Hidden AI breaks it.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 anti-patterns]

Three AI PM anti-patterns.

*Anti-pattern one — chasing model benchmarks over user outcomes.* The model scores well on internal benchmarks but doesn't help users. *Benchmarks are not the product.*

*Anti-pattern two — shipping AI without eval and telemetry.* The team is flying blind. Customer-reported issues are the first signal of problems. *Too late.*

*Anti-pattern three — pushing AI on every feature.* Not every problem is an AI problem. *Adding AI to features that worked fine without it often makes them worse.*
