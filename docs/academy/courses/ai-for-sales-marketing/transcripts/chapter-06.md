> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 tiers]

Three personalisation tiers.

*Tier one — light personalisation.* Name, company, industry, role. Public data. *No consent friction. Universal acceptance.*

*Tier two — middle personalisation.* Recent activity on your site, content downloads, event attendance. *First-party data they gave you knowingly.* Consent in privacy policy. *Acceptable when disclosed.*

*Tier three — deep personalisation.* Cross-source behaviour, intent data, predictive next-need scoring. *Powerful when done right. Crosses the creepy line when done wrong.*

The principle — *use the tier the relationship justifies.* New lead — tier one. Engaged prospect — tier two. Long-term customer with active conversation — tier three.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 creepy-line categories]

Three creepy-line categories. *Don't cross these.*

*Category one — health, financial distress, family events.* AI may detect these signals from behavioural data. *Don't reference them in outreach.* "I noticed you've been searching debt consolidation" is the textbook creepy-line cross.

*Category two — surveillance-level detail.* "I noticed you opened my email at three forty-seven AM" — even if it's true, even if it's their work pattern. *Don't reference it.* It signals surveillance, not relationship.

*Category three — inferred personal characteristics.* AI inferring age, gender, ethnicity, religion, sexuality from behavioural signals. *Bloomberg and Wilson-Caliskan cross-domain — AI inherits biases.* If your AI infers protected categories, you're one regulator letter away from a problem.

The rule. *Relevant beats clever. Use the data they knowingly gave you. Make the message valuable. Stop there.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · consent regime]

The consent regime by jurisdiction.

*GDPR — explicit consent for marketing, granular by purpose, easy to withdraw.* Article Seven. The Article Twenty-Two boundary on automated decisions applies to personalisation too.

*India DPDPA — twenty twenty-three.* Consent before processing sensitive personal data. *Data-fiduciary obligations.* Granular consent. Easy withdrawal.

*CCPA — California.* Opt-out for sale and share. Sensitive personal information has additional protections.

The combined posture. *Three rules.* Granular consent at collection. *Single-click withdrawal at any point.* No selling without explicit opt-in. *If you can't honour those three, don't run the tier-three personalisation play.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · what works]

What works practically. *Three patterns.*

*Pattern one — tier two as the default.* First-party data, declared consent, clear value exchange. *The boring choice. The choice that scales.*

*Pattern two — tier three only for the active relationship.* Existing customer with active conversation. Account-based marketing for stage-three opportunities. *Not for top-of-funnel.*

*Pattern three — disclosure builds trust.* "We use your activity data to make our outreach relevant — change preferences here." *Transparent personalisation outperforms hidden personalisation. Counterintuitive but the data is consistent.*

The Monday move. *Audit your last personalised campaign.* For each personalisation token — *was the data first-party, consented, and value-adding?* If any token is no on any of those — *that's the cleanup work this quarter.*
