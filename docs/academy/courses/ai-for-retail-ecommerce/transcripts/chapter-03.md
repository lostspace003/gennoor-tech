Three pricing AI plays.

*Play one — dynamic pricing within guardrails.* Demand-based adjustments. Competitor-aware. *Constrained by min, max, and brand rules.*

*Play two — markdown optimisation.* End-of-season, end-of-life clearance pricing. *Maximise sell-through while protecting margin.*

*Play three — promotional uplift modelling.* Predicting promo response. Avoiding wasted promotion on already-converting customers.

These three ship reliably. *The implementation discipline determines outcome.*

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the guardrail discipline]

The guardrail discipline. *Non-negotiable.*

For every pricing AI deployment. *Document explicit minimum prices, maximum prices, and brand-consistency rules.* Document the categories where dynamic pricing applies. Document the categories where it does not.

The categories where it should not. *Essentials during emergencies — water during a hurricane, medication during a shortage, fuel during a crisis.* Surge pricing on essentials lands retailers on the news. *FTC and state AGs take notice. So do consumers — and they remember.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:15]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the personalised pricing trap]

The personalised pricing trap.

The temptation. *Show different prices to different customers based on willingness to pay.* The math is compelling. The risk is severe.

The patterns that draw regulatory and consumer ire. *Different price on the same product based on detected income, location, or device.* That is functionally price discrimination — *and increasingly regulated, particularly in California, EU, and emerging India guidance.*

What works instead. *Personalised offers and bundles, not personalised list prices.* Promotional emails, loyalty rewards, app-exclusive offers — these are legal and accepted. *Personalised list prices break trust the moment they're discovered.*
