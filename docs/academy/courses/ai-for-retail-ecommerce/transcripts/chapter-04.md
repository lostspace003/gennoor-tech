> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · 3 personalisation tiers]

The three personalisation tiers.

*Tier one — light personalisation.* Name, recent category browsing, public history. *Universally acceptable.*

*Tier two — middle personalisation.* Recent activity, content downloads, declared preferences. *First-party data they gave you knowingly. Acceptable when disclosed.*

*Tier three — deep personalisation.* Cross-source, inferred preference, predictive next-need. *Powerful when done well. Crosses the creepy line when done wrong.*

Match tier to relationship. *New customer — tier one. Engaged customer — tier two. Long-term loyalty customer with active conversation — tier three.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:15]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the creepy line · 3 categories]

The creepy line in retail. *Three categories. Don't cross.*

*Category one — health, financial distress, family events.* Don't reference these in personalised messaging even if your data shows them. *Detected pregnancy. Recent bereavement. Job loss. Don't personalise on these.*

*Category two — surveillance-level granularity.* "I noticed you visited the store on Tuesday at three p.m." Even if true. *Signals surveillance.*

*Category three — inferred protected characteristics.* Age, ethnicity, religion inferred from behaviour. *Bloomberg and Wilson-Caliskan cross-domain — AI inherits training data bias.* Acting on inferred protected characteristics is one regulator letter away from a problem.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the trust math]

The trust math. *Relevant beats clever.*

*Customer-perceived value of relevant recommendation.* Saves time. Discovers products. Improves experience.

*Customer-perceived value of clever-but-creepy recommendation.* Discomfort. Reduced future trust. Sometimes abandonment.

The retailer's instinct is to push toward clever. *The data supports relevant.* Multiple academic studies and industry surveys consistently show transparency-and-relevance outperforms hidden-and-clever.

What works. *Disclosure of why a recommendation is offered. Easy opt-out from personalisation. Honour the opt-out within the system, not just at the surface.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · honest scoring]

How to score personalisation honestly. *Two questions.*

*Question one — for last quarter's personalised messaging, what's the negative-feedback rate — unsubscribe, complaint, "this is creepy"?* Under one percent green. One to three amber. *Above three percent red — you've crossed the line for a meaningful share of customers.*

*Question two — when a customer opts out of personalisation, is the opt-out honoured across all touchpoints — email, app, on-site, ads?* Yes green. Mostly amber. *Honestly no red.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *Audit one personalised campaign.* For each token used — is the data first-party, consented, and value-adding? *Any "no" → cleanup work this quarter.*
