Chapter six. *Sustaining momentum past year one.* Most companies have a strong year one of AI investment — pilots, enthusiasm, executive support. *Year two is where it breaks.* This chapter is why, and what to do about it.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the year-2 trap]

The year-two trap. *Pilot proliferation. No scaling.* Year one — six pilots launched. Year two — six more pilots launched. None of the first six scaled. None of the next six will either. *The organisation is now running twelve underfunded experiments and none has the resources to actually move the business.*

Why this happens. *Killing a pilot feels like failure.* Executive sponsors fight to keep their pilots alive. *The CoE doesn't have authority to kill.* Capital spreads thinner. Outcomes deteriorate.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · fix 1]

Fix one. *Kill two of every three pilots at the end of year one.* No exceptions. *Even ones with executive sponsorship.* This is the most important capital-allocation move in year two.

The AI council from chapter four owns this decision. *Stage-gate criteria — did the pilot pass its measurement framework?* If yes — fund to scale. If no — kill cleanly. Don't extend.

The optics. *Killing pilots looks like setback. It's actually concentration.* Communicate that. *The two pilots you keep get five times the resources they had. That's how scaling actually happens.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · fix 2]

Fix two. *Scale one with discipline.* The pilot that passes goes to ten times the user base. *Not by adding ten times the marketing.* By embedding it in the core workflow.

What "embed" means. *The tool is the default.* People don't have to remember to use it. *It's the first thing that opens in their morning workflow.* Friction is engineered out. The Microsoft Work Trend Index thirty-percent productivity gain — *only kicks in for active users. Embedding creates active users.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:55]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · fix 3]

Fix three. *Make AI-augmented work the default.* Not the optional shiny tool. *The expected baseline.*

This requires manager-level reinforcement. *Performance conversations include AI fluency.* New hires are evaluated on AI-augmented productivity. *Output expectations are calibrated to AI-augmented baselines, not pre-AI baselines.* This is uncomfortable. It's also how durable adoption happens.

Anthropic's Responsible Scaling Policy and OpenAI's Preparedness Framework — *both vendors published safety frameworks for frontier model release between twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-five.* Worth knowing. *The frontier-model layer is being actively governed by vendors.* Your job is the application layer. Both layers matter.

The Monday move. *At your next AI council meeting, the agenda includes one item — "which pilots are we killing this quarter?"* No agenda is complete without it. *The council that doesn't kill anything is not exercising capital discipline. It's running theatre.*
