Chapter four. *Talent and organisation design.* You can have the right capital allocation and still fail if the org isn't designed to absorb AI. *Three talent moves. One structural pattern. The CoE trap.*

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · WEF context]

The WEF context first. *World Economic Forum Future of Jobs Report twenty twenty-five.* Forty percent of employers expect AI and automation to reduce parts of the workforce by twenty thirty. *Eighty-six percent expect to transform their workforce through AI and automation.* These two numbers are not contradictory — *some reduction, mostly transformation.*

Translated for your strategy. *The work of the median employee in your company will change materially over the next twenty-four to thirty-six months.* The companies that plan for that win. The companies that don't are managing a downstream productivity crisis.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:10]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the 3 talent moves]

The three talent moves. *Sequenced.*

*Move one — identify AI-native operators within your existing workforce.* These are the employees who, given a free Copilot license, are using it for two-thirds of their drafting and analysis within a month. *They self-identify if you create the conditions.* Find them. Give them more.

*Move two — upskill the existing senior layer.* Mid-level and senior managers who run teams. *Without them, AI adoption stalls at the individual level.* Two-day immersions plus monthly office hours. Not a one-off training.

*Move three — hire a small specialist team.* Five to ten people. *Machine learning, applied AI, AI product management.* Not a department of fifty. *A specialist team that supports the rest of the org.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:25]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the CoE trap]

The Centre of Excellence trap. *Most large companies set up an AI CoE in twenty twenty-three or twenty twenty-four.* By twenty twenty-six, the data is clear — *pure CoEs fail.*

Why. *Because the CoE owns the AI but not the business.* The business owns the P-and-L but not the AI. *Decision authority and accountability split.* Initiatives stall in the middle.

The fix — *the AI council pattern.* A small body — eight to twelve people — that includes the AI specialist team plus business-unit leaders. *Meets every two weeks.* Sets priorities. Approves investment. Kills failing pilots.

The CoE if you keep it — *narrow it.* It owns standards, tooling, infrastructure. It doesn't own outcomes. *Business units own outcomes.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · structural design]

The structural pattern. *Hub-and-spoke with a council.* The hub — small specialist team. *The spokes — embedded AI-native operators in each business unit.* The council — joint decision authority.

What this avoids. *The fully-centralised model where AI strategy doesn't reach the front line.* The fully-decentralised model where every business unit reinvents the wheel. *The hub-and-spoke captures the standardisation benefits without losing local context.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]

The Monday move. *In the next thirty days — name the AI council members.* Eight to twelve people. Right mix of specialist plus business-unit. *First meeting agenda — review the current pilot list, prioritise three, set kill-criteria for the rest.* If you can't get that meeting in the calendar, *the org doesn't yet have the structure to absorb AI strategy.*
