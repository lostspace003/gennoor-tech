Rule one. *Concentrate.* From chapter two — *pick two of the four value patterns. Not all four.* Within those two patterns, pick three to five use cases. *Not twenty.*

The BCG and MIT data is unambiguous. *Leaders capture one-and-a-half to two times the growth of laggards because they concentrated.* Laggards spread thin and got under-water on every initiative.

The practical move. *If your AI portfolio has more than seven active initiatives across the business, you're already in the laggard cohort.* Cut to three. Re-evaluate the cuts at the next quarterly review.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · rule 2]

Rule two. *Stage-gate.* Not all-in upfront.

The pattern. *Each initiative goes through three gates.* Pilot gate — *prove it works in one team for one quarter.* Scale gate — *prove the productivity or revenue claim holds at ten times the user base.* Embed gate — *prove the team uses it without prompting after three months.*

Each gate has a kill-or-fund decision. *If a pilot doesn't pass its gate, you kill it cleanly.* No "let's keep it running a bit longer." That's how capital evaporates.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · rule 3]

Rule three. *Measure.* Deloitte's twenty twenty-five AI surveys — *only twenty-five percent of organisations have measurement frameworks tied to AI initiatives.* Three-quarters are flying blind. *Be in the twenty-five percent.*

What the framework needs. *Baseline before — what was the time, cost, or accuracy before AI.* Counterfactual — what would have happened without the AI investment. *Quality controls — re-work, verification, customer satisfaction.* Net delta — what was the actual value capture.

This is not exotic. *It's just rigour applied consistently.* Most AI initiatives skip it because measurement is unglamorous. The companies that measure win the capital-allocation conversation at the next board meeting.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the close]
