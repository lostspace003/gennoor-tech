> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the principle]

The principle. *Calibration over confidence.* From Tetlock's twenty-plus years of research — *the person who says "I'm seventy percent confident and right seventy percent of the time" is more trustworthy than the one who says "I'm certain and right eighty percent."* Apply this to your AI reporting.

When you tell the board "AI saved three thousand hours this quarter," the board's question is — *how do you know.* Have the answer ready. *That's calibration.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the 5 metrics]

The five metrics on the one-page board AI dashboard.

*Metric one — investment.* Capital deployed to AI initiatives this quarter and year-to-date. *Broken out by value pattern.*

*Metric two — productivity.* Measured time savings or revenue impact. *Not "users feel productive." Measured.*

*Metric three — risk register.* Open issues. New issues. Resolved issues. *Bias monitoring results. Customer complaints.*

*Metric four — regulatory posture.* EU AI Act readiness. NIST AI RMF alignment. State-level compliance status.

*Metric five — talent.* AI council attendance. Specialist team headcount. Training completion rates.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the Tetlock discipline]

The Tetlock discipline applied. *Every productivity number on the dashboard has a confidence range.* "Thirty percent productivity gain on Copilot users" — *with what error bars?* Was the measurement a controlled experiment, an A-B test, a survey, or a vendor study?

The honest dashboard names the measurement quality. *Strong evidence — green.* Suggestive evidence — *amber.* Vendor claim only — *red, requires validation.* This is unusual. It builds board trust. *Because the board has been pitched too many over-confident AI numbers in too many quarterly reviews.*
