> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Chapter two. *The four AI value patterns.* If you're going to allocate capital to AI, you need to know what kind of value you're allocating against. *Four patterns. Most CEOs over-index on one and ignore the other three.* This chapter is which one you should pick.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:15]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 1]

Pattern one. *Productivity.* The most-cited value pattern. *MIT, BCG, Harvard, Goldman Sachs through twenty twenty-three and twenty twenty-four — fifteen to forty percent productivity gains on knowledge-work tasks for AI-augmented workers.* Microsoft Work Trend Index — *around thirty percent time savings on routine documents for active Copilot users.*

What "productivity" means in practice. *Drafting, summarising, classifying, translating, code generation.* The white-collar work that fills knowledge workers' days. AI compresses the time it takes. *The savings compound over months — if and only if the team actually uses it consistently.*

The trap. *Productivity gains are real but easy to over-claim.* Measure carefully. *Chapter seven on the audit committee question.*

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:20]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 2]

Pattern two. *Products.* AI as a feature in your customer-facing offering. *Salesforce Agentforce in customer service. Microsoft Sales Agent. Intercom Fin. Zendesk AI.* Or your own product team building AI features into what you ship.

This is where the largest revenue upside sits. *And the largest risk surface.* The Air Canada Moffatt case — chatbot promising a refund the airline didn't offer — is the cautionary tale. *A product-facing AI failure becomes the company's statement.*

Pick this pattern if your business model is product-led. *Then invest in the controls — chapter five on governance.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:20]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 3]

Pattern three. *Decisions.* AI in forecasting, pricing, routing, classification, risk-scoring. The places where structured data plus a model produces better-than-human predictions on specific narrow tasks. *Demand forecasting in supply chain. Credit decisioning in finance. Customer churn prediction in CS. Pricing dynamics in commercial.*

The thing to know. *ML-on-structured-data has been delivering value since long before generative AI.* The pattern is mature. The risks are well understood — bias, model drift, governance. *Bloomberg's resume study from March twenty twenty-four is the cautionary tale on decisions involving people.*

This pattern is the most defensible to your board. *Specific. Measurable. Decades of methodology.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:25]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · pattern 4]

Pattern four. *Automation.* AI orchestrating workflows end-to-end. Document processing. Invoice handling. Compliance reporting. *The agentic pattern that's emerging through twenty twenty-five and twenty twenty-six.*

Where it's working. *Back-office workflows with clear inputs, clear outputs, and clear hand-offs.* Where it's not working yet — *open-ended customer interactions, complex judgement calls, anything regulated where wrong answers cost money.*

The frontier. *Agentic AI is the most-pitched pattern of twenty twenty-six and the least-mature.* Your board will hear a lot about it. *Approach with the same discipline as the other three. Pilot, measure, decide.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the portfolio question]

The portfolio question. *Pick two of the four for the next twenty-four months. Not all four.* The temptation to pursue everything is what kills capital discipline.

*Productivity is the safest bet — broadest applicability.* Products is the highest-upside bet — and the highest risk. *Decisions is the most-defensible bet to the board.* Automation is the bet of the future — choose if your industry permits it.
