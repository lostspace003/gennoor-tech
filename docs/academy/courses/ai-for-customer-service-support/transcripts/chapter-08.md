Adoption with frontline teams. *Where most CS-AI rollouts succeed or fail.*

Your agents are the users. *If they trust the AI, they use it well. If they do not, they ignore it — and the productivity gain disappears.*

What builds frontline trust. *Three moves.*

*Move one — agent training before launch.* Not five-minute video. Hands-on. Specific scenarios. Real coaching. *One day minimum per role.*

*Move two — champions in each team.* Two or three power-user agents who become the in-team support. *They diffuse the literacy faster than any vendor training program.*

*Move three — listen to agent feedback weekly.* What is working? What is annoying? What is missing? *Act on the feedback visibly so agents know they are heard.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 12-week rollout]

The twelve-week rollout.

*Weeks one to four — agent assist for tier-one queries.* Foundation. Lowest risk. Highest agent adoption.

*Weeks five to eight — scoped deflection bot for top three high-volume queries.* Strong escalation. KB discipline in place. Monitoring active.

*Weeks nine to twelve — sentiment and escalation triggers in production.* QA loop running. Coaching cadence established. Voice assist piloted.

By end of week twelve. *Foundation in place. Discipline established. Customer experience metrics moving in the right direction.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 trust trip-wires]

Four trust trip-wires. *Do not cross.*

*Trip-wire one — autonomous bots without strong grounding.* Air Canada Moffatt. NYC MyCity. The next public failure is loaded.

*Trip-wire two — AI gate-keeping access to humans.* Customers reach humans on reasonable request. Always.

*Trip-wire three — voice AI without disclosure or consent.* Regulatory action and reputational damage compounding.

*Trip-wire four — full bot replacement of human support.* The Klarna lesson. Customer lifetime value is not worth the year-one cost saving.
