> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · gaming]

The gaming risk. *Harvard Business School working paper thirteen-zero-seven-three — Larkin, on employee gaming in enterprise software sales.* The finding — *enterprise software reps shift deal timing and pricing in response to commission accelerators.* When the plan rewards sandbagged Q4 to load Q1, reps sandbag Q4. When the plan rewards bigger deals, reps inflate quote prices to hit accelerator thresholds. *The plan optimises for the metric, the rep optimises against the plan.*

This is what AI compensation modelling has to stress-test for. *Before you ship the plan, simulate the rep's optimal response.* If the simulated rep ends up gaming the metric — pulling forward deals, inflating size, delaying discounting — *the plan rewards behaviour you don't want.* Revise before launch.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:25]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · what to check]

What to stress-test specifically. *Three patterns.* One — *accelerator thresholds.* What does the rep do near the threshold? Sandbag below or push above? Two — *quarterly cliffs.* What happens in week thirteen of Q4? Three — *cross-product attribution.* If the rep gets credit for two products on one deal, does that change which product gets sold? *Each of these has caused real revenue distortion in real companies.*
