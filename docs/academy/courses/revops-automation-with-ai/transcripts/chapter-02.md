The trap that kills most rollouts. *Rep revolt.* Gartner predicted in November 2025 that by 2028 AI agents will outnumber sellers ten-to-one — and *fewer than forty percent of sellers will report AI agents improved their productivity.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · nudge design]

Nudge design that actually gets actioned. *Five rules.* One — *one nudge per deal per week, maximum.* Two — *include the specific evidence in the nudge.* Not "this deal is at risk" — "*this deal has been in proposal stage for forty-five days, push count three, no activity in twelve days.*" Three — *give the rep the one-click action.* Update stage. Schedule next step. Defer for fourteen days with a reason. Four — *route to the manager, not just the rep, when push count exceeds three.* The pattern at that point is usually a deal that needs sales-leadership intervention, not rep effort. Five — *measure act-vs-dismiss rate per nudge type.* Kill any nudge type with a dismiss rate above seventy percent. *The nudge isn't earning its slot in the rep's attention budget.*

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:35]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the sorter]

The interactive on screen. Five nudge designs. *Pick which ones reps will action vs dismiss.* The pattern that emerges — specificity, single-click action, frequency cap. Generic nudges with multiple steps get dismissed. *Every. Single. Time.*

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · pattern]

The cross-cutting principle. *Don't compete with the rep's attention. Earn it.* Every nudge needs to pass the test — *would I send this Slack DM to the rep myself?* If the answer is no, the AI shouldn't either.

Refusal rate drops to under ten percent when the proposed update includes the source quote.
