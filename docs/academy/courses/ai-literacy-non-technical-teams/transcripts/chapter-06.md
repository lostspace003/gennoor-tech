Okay. Last one.

Five chapters of vocabulary, prompting, function lanes, risk, a library.
None of that matters if the week after this course you forget every
single thing and never open a Copilot window again. Which is what
happens to most people.

So this chapter is the meta-skill. How to make this actually stick at
your desk — past Tuesday. Real action, not motivation.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:30]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the number]

I'm going to start with a number that ought to bother you. In surveys
of people who tried Copilot at work and then *stopped* using it —
forty-four-point-two percent gave the same reason. *"I didn't trust the
answer."* Just under half.

> [S2 ▸ reveal 2 · the second number]

And the broader picture is worse. Gartner — only twenty-four percent of
Copilot pilots ever expand beyond twenty percent of the workforce. Among
employees who have BOTH Copilot and ChatGPT available, Copilot adoption
drops to eighteen percent. ChatGPT gets seventy-six.

These aren't stats about whether AI is good or bad. They're stats about
*adoption*. About whether the human at the desk decided this thing was
worth their second attempt. Most decided no. After one try.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:35]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · the trap named]

Here's what's actually happening. I call it the week-one trap. Almost
everyone goes through it.

Monday — you try AI on a task. The output is mediocre. *Decent, but I
could have done it.* So you put it aside.

Tuesday — you try again. Same kind of result. You start thinking *maybe
this isn't for me*. Maybe you don't have the right kind of work for it.

Wednesday — you stop trying. You go back to doing things the way you did
last month. And six weeks later, when your manager asks how the Copilot
rollout is going for you — *yeah, I tried it, it didn't really help*.
Roll-out stalls. Forty-four percent.

> [S3 ▸ reveal 2 · why the trap works]

Now — here's what's actually wrong with that story. The first three
prompts anyone writes are bad. That's not a *you* problem. That's a
*calibration* problem. Everyone goes through it. The good prompts come
later — after you've felt the difference between a vague prompt and a
structured one. After you've kept a few that worked. After you have a
library.

The trap is that we quit before the calibration loop has had time to
work. We mistake *being early* for *being a bad fit*.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the week-one cadence]

So — let me tell you what the actual first week should look like. Real
days. Real moves.

> [pause briefly · ≈ 2 seconds]

Monday — one prompt. From the library. On a task that's already on your
calendar. Fifteen minutes. Don't pick the hardest task you have. Pick
something concrete you know how to do, where you can recognise the
difference between a six and an eight.

Tuesday — same prompt, different material. Same kind of task. Different
content. You're not testing the prompt. You're testing *whether this
prompt works in your hands, on your typical inputs*.

Wednesday — the prompt that didn't quite work yesterday. Now you change
one thing. Add a constraint. Paste a different source. Whatever you
remember from chapter two. *One change.* Don't tear it apart. Tune it.

Thursday — a different prompt from the library. Different task. Different
function pattern. So you've now tried two prompts, on two kinds of work.
Three runs each. Six data points.

Friday — fifteen minutes of writing. Two sentences each: which prompt
worked, what I'd change, when I'd use it again. *That's* what becomes
your library. Not the prompts themselves. The notes.
