# Chapter 4 — The risks you should know

**Course:** AI Literacy for Non-Technical Teams · **Chapter:** 04 · **Target
duration:** ~6 min spoken · **Word count target:** ~1,000 words

## Trainer persona

Same voice. This chapter is the most serious of the six — three real
incidents, with real money, in three minutes. Don't joke through these.
Don't moralise either. Read them like a senior practitioner who has
watched these things happen and wants new users to not make the same
mistakes.

Body below is the spoken transcript. Cue markers map to slide / step

> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Okay — the chapter you were warned about. The risk chapter. And the
reason it has its own chapter, instead of being a footnote somewhere, is
that the way this goes wrong in 2026 is now public, named, and recorded
in court rulings. I'm going to tell you three of them. Then I'm going to
tell you what each one means for you, sitting at your desk, on a Tuesday.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:30]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · Air Canada]

Story one. Air Canada, 2024. A passenger's grandmother dies. He goes to
the Air Canada website. He asks the chatbot whether bereavement fares can
be claimed retroactively. The chatbot says yes — book the flight first,
claim the discount within ninety days. So he does. Then Air Canada
refuses the refund. So he takes them to the British Columbia Civil
Resolution Tribunal.

> [S2 ▸ reveal 2 · the defence]

Now — here's the part I want you to remember. Air Canada's defence —
this is in the ruling — was that the chatbot is *"a separate legal
entity"*. That Air Canada couldn't be liable for what the chatbot said.

The tribunal's response — word for word — *"While a chatbot has an
interactive component, it is still just a part of Air Canada's website."*
Air Canada lost. They were ordered to pay eight hundred and twelve
Canadian dollars. Eight hundred. That's the headline. But the dollar
amount isn't the story. The story is the precedent. Anything your
company's AI says — your company said.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:45]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · iTutor]

Story two. iTutorGroup. A tutoring company. 2020 — they configured their
recruitment software to auto-reject women over fifty-five and men over
sixty. Just — *gone*, before a human ever read the application. Over two
hundred qualified U.S. applicants screened out by a rule someone
configured.

> [S3 ▸ reveal 2 · the settlement]

The EEOC sued. The first AI-bias lawsuit the agency ever filed. iTutor
settled in 2023 for three hundred and sixty-five thousand dollars. Plus
they had to invite every rejected applicant to reapply, run
anti-discrimination training, and tell the EEOC every time someone now
complains.

The thing to notice — and this is the one that catches people — isn't
that the software was biased. Software does what it's told. The thing to
notice is that *someone configured that filter*. And when the lawyers
showed up, *the AI did it* wasn't a defence. The accountability follows
whoever set the rule. Always.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · Samsung]

Story three. Samsung. April 2023.

Samsung let their engineers start using ChatGPT for work. Three weeks
later — three separate incidents. One engineer pasted proprietary source
code in, asking for help debugging. One pasted a full confidential
meeting transcript, asking for a summary. One pasted yield-test sequences
for chip-defect identification.

Three weeks. Samsung banned generative AI on company devices. Apple
followed. JPMorgan. Verizon. Amazon. All within months.

> [S4 ▸ reveal 2 · the takeaway]

When I tell you what not to paste — these aren't hypotheticals. These
are the things that already cost a major company its leverage in three
weeks flat.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:15]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · the red lines]

So here's the rule. Don't memorise this as a vibe — memorise it as a
list. Things that go in a public AI tool, and things that don't.

Don't paste: customer personal data. Don't paste: salary data. Don't
paste: anything under an NDA. Don't paste: a board pack before the board
has seen it. Don't paste: a draft of someone's PIP. Don't paste: source
code that isn't already open. Don't paste: contracts.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the carve-out]

The carve-out is important. If your company has *enterprise* Copilot or
*enterprise* ChatGPT — and you've confirmed it — most of these rules
relax. Microsoft's published statement is direct, you can quote it back —
*"Prompts, responses, and data accessed through Microsoft Graph aren't
used to train foundation LLMs."* Your enterprise data stays inside your
tenant. That's the deal you're paying for.

## Notes for the recording session

- Pace the three incident stories deliberately. Each one ends on a
  landing line — *"Anything your company's AI says, your company said."*
  *"The accountability follows whoever set the rule."* *"Three weeks
  flat."* These are the moments learners remember.
- No moralising tone. These are facts. The trainer is a colleague who
  has watched this play out, not a compliance officer.
- Slide 6 — 15s hands-on pause for the sorter interactive. Confirm.
