> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Okay — promised you the fun one. Here we are.

This chapter is a library. Ten prompts I've used, tested, fixed, used
again, and stopped trying to remember from scratch each time. Because
that's the actual move once you've been at this a few months — you stop
re-inventing prompts. You collect them. You tune them. You reach for the
one that matches the shape of today's task.

The point of this chapter isn't for me to read ten prompts to you. That
would be deeply boring, and you'd remember none of them. The point is for
you to *take three* and run them this week. The interactive on slide 3
has all ten. You'll pick yours.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the reframe]

Quick reframe first. Most people who try AI for work treat each prompt as
a fresh thing. They type something, get back something, copy-paste it,
move on. Three months later, they've written four hundred prompts and
they remember none of them. The good ones got lost. The bad ones —
they're typing variations of them again right now.

> [S2 ▸ reveal 2 · the library effect]

People who get real lift do something different. They keep the prompts
that worked. Anywhere — Notes app, Notion, a sticky on the monitor,
doesn't matter. The first month, they have maybe five. By month three,
twenty. By six months, the library is doing the heavy lift, and they're
just *editing* prompts that already work, not writing new ones from
scratch.

Anthropic's research from earlier this year — the Economic Index report —
shows experienced users have a ten percent higher success rate, after
controlling for task type. That's the library effect. Compounding.

Right. The library.

Ten prompts on the screen. Each one is a card. Click any to expand —

> [S3 ▸ reveal 2 · narrate three]

Let me walk you through three of them. The other seven you'll browse.

> [pause for hands-on or trainer-walks · ≈ 8 seconds]

First one — summarising meeting notes into action items. Goal: extract
who-owes-what-by-when from a recording transcript. The thing that makes
this prompt work isn't the summary part. The model can summarise
anything. It's the *output format* — "table with three columns: Owner,
Action, Due-by". That structural constraint forces the model to commit.
Without it, you get prose. With it, you get a list someone could send.

Second — drafting a follow-up email from sparse notes. We covered this
in chapter two. The library version is more polished — it includes a
sample-prospect note pattern so you can drop your real material into the
same slot every time. Reusable.

Third — and this is the one that surprises people — *audit a document
for jargon*. Paste in a draft. Ask the model to flag every term a
non-specialist wouldn't understand, AND propose a plain-English
replacement, AND keep the technical accuracy. Three constraints, all
explicit. This prompt has saved a lot of board memos from being
incomprehensible.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · the pattern]

If you look at all ten — which you should, browse the panel — you'll
notice they have the same shape. Goal. Context. Source. Expectations.

Same pattern from chapter two. Always. The library isn't a collection of
*tricks*. It's a collection of well-applied versions of the same pattern,
tuned to specific work.

> [S4 ▸ reveal 2 · the meta-tip]

There's one more thing worth saying about the library. Every prompt that
ends up in yours should have a note attached. *Why this one works.* Two
sentences. *"The output format constraint is what makes this." "Pasting
the actual past-emails-source is what makes this."* You'll forget — fast
— if you don't write the *why* down.

When you do that, the library doesn't just contain prompts. It contains
*your understanding of what good prompting looks like*. That's the
asset.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:30]

One thing to do before chapter six.

Pick three of the ten. Not all ten. Three. Pick ones that map to tasks

sample inputs.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the feedback loop]

Now the bit that turns this into a library and not a list. After you

you'd do differently. Two seconds of writing. Compounds enormously over
the year. By chapter six, you'll have three prompts in your library, with
notes. That's a real start.
