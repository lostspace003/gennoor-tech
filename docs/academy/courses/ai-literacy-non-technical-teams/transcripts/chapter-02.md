# Chapter 2 — Prompting as a learnable skill

**Course:** AI Literacy for Non-Technical Teams · **Chapter:** 02 · **Target
duration:** ~7 min spoken (≈ 10 min with slide-boundary silence) ·
**Word count target:** ~1,200 words

## Trainer persona

Same voice as chapter 1. 12-year practitioner who has deployed AI into
non-technical teams. Dry. Self-deprecating about her own early prompts —
*especially* in this chapter, since it's literally about her own bad
prompt. Region-neutral. Conversational pace ~150–160 wpm.

## Transcript

> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Right. Last chapter we sorted out the three kinds of AI. Now — let's
actually talk to one. Specifically the generative kind. The chat box. The
*Help me write* button. The thing that makes most people either fall in
love with AI or write it off completely, depending on the first prompt
they tried.

And here's the bit I want you to leave with. The difference between
getting a six-out-of-ten answer from one of these tools — and getting a
nine — almost all of that is in how you prompted. Not the tool. Not the
model. The prompt. Same chat box, same model, two completely different
results. Which means this is a skill. Which means you can get better at
it. Which is what we're doing in the next seven minutes.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:55]

So I'm going to show you a real prompt. I wrote this last Tuesday. I'm
not proud of it. Here it is.

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · the bad prompt]

*Help me write a follow-up email.*

That's it. That's what I typed. Eight words. And I got back — a follow-up
email. Polite, formatted, perfectly fine English. Could've been from
anyone. Could've been to anyone. Could've been about literally anything.
Six out of ten.

> [S2 ▸ reveal 2 · the trap]

And this is the trap with weak prompting. The thing that comes back looks
like an answer. It's grammatical. It's the right length. So if you're not
paying attention, you just copy it and send it. And then later you tell a
colleague — *yeah I tried AI, it's fine, didn't really save me time.*
Which is basically what most people say when their prompts are this thin.

So — watch what happens when I make three changes. Just three.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:00]

First change.

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · Fix 1 — Context]

I tell it who I am and what's actually going on. So I add — *I'm a sales
rep. I had a discovery call yesterday with a CIO at a mid-sized logistics
company. We talked for forty minutes. They asked good questions. I need
to follow up today.*

I haven't even told it what to write yet. I've just told it who's
speaking and what just happened. And the email that comes back — totally
different. Specific. Names the kind of company. References the kind of
conversation. We're at maybe an eight, just from that.

What changed? It knows who's talking now. Which means it can write in MY
voice — sales rep, post-discovery — instead of a generic everybody voice.
And generic-everybody is the worst voice in business writing.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

Second change.

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · Fix 2 — Source]

I tell it where to look. Because right now, even with the context, it's
still making up the actual details. So I add — *here are my notes from the
call* — and I paste in three bullets. Their main pain point. The two
questions they kept circling back to. The next step we said we'd take.

Now it's not guessing. It's working from MY notes. The email it produces
mentions their actual pain point, by name. References the actual
questions they asked. We're at eight-and-a-half.

This bit is important. The single biggest reason AI hallucinates — the
thing people complain about all the time — is that we didn't give it the
real source. We asked it to invent something. So it did. If you can hand
it the actual email thread, the actual meeting notes, the actual policy
document — accuracy goes up dramatically. Because it stops guessing.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:10]

Third change.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · Fix 3 — Expectations]

I tell it what good looks like. Format, length, tone. So I add — *three
paragraphs, friendly but professional, don't push for a meeting in this
one, and end with a question they can answer in one line.*

Now — look at what I just did. I just told it — be brief, be warm, don't
be pushy, make it easy to reply. Those are the four things I would have
asked a JUNIOR to think about before sending the email. I just wrote them
into the prompt instead. The model can't read my mind. But it can read my
brief. So if I write down what I'd say to a junior — it does most of what
a junior would do.

Now we're at a nine. Maybe nine-and-a-half. From the same starting point.
Same model. Same chat window. Three sentences added.

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:15]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · framework named]

Okay so — here's the thing. What we just did is a pattern. You can write
it on a Post-it.

The original ask — *help me write a follow-up email* — that's a Goal.
Goals on their own are necessary. They are not sufficient.

Then I added three things. Context — who I am, what just happened.
Source — my actual notes. Expectations — what the output should look
like.

Goal. Context. Source. Expectations. Four words. That's the pattern.
Microsoft uses it inside their Copilot guidance. Other vendors use the
same idea, different labels. Same four parts.

> [S6 ▸ reveal 2 · interactive panel]

And here's the thing about it. It's not a checklist you fill in every
single time. A quick spell-check fix? Just a Goal. *Make this sentence
shorter*? Just a Goal. But if you're getting weak output — and you will,
plenty of times — these are the three things to check. Did I tell it who
I am? Did I give it the source? Did I say what the output should look
like? Almost always, one of those three is missing.

Try the panel on the screen. Toggle the three fixes on and off. See how
the output changes when each one is applied. You'll feel the lift each
addition gives you.

> [pause for hands-on — ≈ 10 seconds of silence]

> [S7 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 7:00]

> [S7 ▸ reveal 1 · the assignment]

Right. So — one thing to do before chapter three.

Find an email you wrote last week. One that took you longer than five
minutes — that's how you know it was the kind of thing where AI could
have helped. Re-do it. Use this pattern. Context, source, expectations.

> [S7 ▸ reveal 2 · the comparison]

Then compare. The original you actually sent, and the new one. Which is
better? Probably the new one. *How* much better? That's the question I
want you to actually have an opinion on by Friday. Because the only way
you know whether this skill is worth investing in — is to feel the lift
yourself, on your own work, this week.

> [S7 ▸ reveal 3 · close]

That's it. Go find one email. See you in chapter three.

> [end]
