# Chapter 3 — Where AI actually helps you (by function)

**Course:** AI Literacy for Non-Technical Teams · **Chapter:** 03 · **Target
duration:** ~8 min spoken · **Word count target:** ~1,300 words

## Trainer persona

Same voice as chapters 1 & 2. Practitioner. Dry. Region-neutral. This
chapter pivots through four functions back to back — pace each one like
you're a colleague *from that function* talking to peers. Don't get
academic.

## Transcript

> [S1 ▸ slide active · t = 0.0]

Right. Two chapters in. You know the three kinds of AI, and you know the
four-part prompt pattern. Now the question gets more practical. *Where
does this actually help me — at my desk — given the function I work in?*

And the honest answer is, it depends on the function. Generative AI is
good at very specific shapes of task. It's wildly less useful for some
things you might assume it'd be great at. So this chapter is four
function lanes — HR, Marketing, Sales, Operations. One concrete story
each. Then a panel you can use to dig deeper into whichever one is yours.

> [S2 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 0:50]

> [S2 ▸ reveal 1 · five patterns named]

Before the four lanes — one thing that's true across all of them. The
useful applications of generative AI mostly fall into five shapes.
Summarize. Draft. Classify. Extract. Transform. That's it. If you can
phrase what you want as one of those five — *summarize these reviews*,
*draft this email*, *classify these tickets*, *extract dates from these
invoices*, *transform this policy into an FAQ* — you're in the
green-light zone.

If you can't fit your task into one of those five, the AI probably can't
help you yet. Knowing this saves you from frustrating prompts that ask
for things the tool can't deliver.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:35]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · HR story]

Function one. HR.

There's an example I keep coming back to, because it's so concrete. A
multinational retailer — five thousand employees — had been collecting
exit-interview data for years. Some of it sat in their Workday forms,
some of it lived in PDF notes a manager had emailed themselves, some of
it was in a survey tool nobody had migrated off.

The HR team had read it. Manually. The way you'd read it. Notes on a few
hundred a year. And they'd told leadership *"we're seeing turnover
pressure, but the picture is mixed."* Which is what people say when
they've read the comments but can't summarize across them.

> [S3 ▸ reveal 2 · the AI did what]

A new HR analyst ran the lot through an AI summarizer. Two hours of
prompt-and-review. What came back was this — *workload and scheduling
issues are heavily concentrated in junior staff, in two specific
regions, in the last six months.* That sentence had been hiding in the
data for a year. It had never been surfaced because nobody had ever read
all of it at once. The AI did what a tireless analyst would do — and it
did it in an afternoon. That's a real outcome. That's HR.

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 3:00]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · Marketing story]

Function two. Marketing.

Adore Me — direct-to-consumer brand, lingerie company, well-known on the
DTC scene. They had a content problem that's familiar to anyone in
marketing. They needed product descriptions for thousands of items.
Different sizes, different colours, different feature combinations. The
copywriting team was the bottleneck. New launches were taking months.

> [S4 ▸ reveal 2 · the numbers]

They built an AI workflow on top of one of the writing platforms — Writer
in this case — to generate the descriptions. Trained it on their brand
voice, gave it the SEO keywords they wanted to enforce, and let it draft.
The numbers came back, and this is on Writer's customer-story page so you
can verify them yourself.

Product description time — twenty hours per batch — down to twenty
minutes. Same batch. Same quality bar. And then the interesting bit.
Non-branded SEO traffic up forty percent. Because they could be
deliberate about which search terms ended up in every description,
consistently, in their voice. Stylist note writing — down thirty-six
percent. Localised launches that used to take months — ten days.

This is marketing where the bottleneck wasn't creativity. It was
*throughput*. AI is excellent at that specific shape.

> [S5 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 4:30]

> [S5 ▸ reveal 1 · Sales story]

Function three. Sales.

The clearest signal in sales — and this is across multiple published
2025 reports, not one outlier — is on outbound personalisation. The
average lift in cold-email response rate, when reps use AI to personalise
based on the prospect's actual public data, is around twenty-eight
percent.

> [S5 ▸ reveal 2 · the pipeline number]

And pipeline lift among teams that use AI well — somewhere between ten
and twenty-five percent. That's a real number. That's the difference
between hitting and missing quota for some teams.

What's actually happening here? The AI isn't writing better emails than
the best human SDR. It's writing better emails than the AVERAGE human
SDR — at scale, personalised to the prospect, in seconds. And that
shifts the median, which shifts the pipeline. *Median is what shows up in
the numbers.*

> [S6 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 5:45]

> [S6 ▸ reveal 1 · Ops story]

Function four. Operations.

A food-processing manufacturer was running invoice intake the way most
mid-sized companies do. A small AP team. Twelve days average to process
an invoice from receipt to system entry. PDF in the email, type into the
ERP, route for approval. Twelve days.

> [S6 ▸ reveal 2 · the result]

They added AI invoice extraction. Read the PDF. Pull the line items.
Pre-fill the entry. Route based on rules they already had. Twelve days
went to two. Processing cost down seventy percent. That kind of number
sounds made up. It isn't. The work hadn't gone away — it had just stopped
being keystrokes-by-humans.

There's a second story from operations worth telling fast. A mid-sized
automotive components manufacturer ran AI across their spend data. Found
out twenty-three percent of their suppliers were actually different
divisions of just eight parent companies. They'd been negotiating with
each in isolation. Consolidating cut spend by eighteen percent in twelve
months. A pattern that was invisible until something tireless read all
the supplier records at once.
