The principle.

Board KPIs are different from team KPIs. *Boards want few, big, defensible. Not 25 dashboards.*

The trap most CDOs fall into. *Reporting data quality scores, lineage coverage percentages, catalog completeness.* Directors don't act on these. They tune them out.

The discipline. *Translate data work into business outcomes the board acts on.* That's how the CDO seat earns its budget.

> [S3 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 1:00]

> [S3 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 board KPIs]

Four KPIs that work at board level.

*KPI one — AI-feature time-to-value.* How long from "AI use case approved" to "in production with measurable impact." Trending down. *Directors understand velocity. Trending up is a credible alarm.*

*KPI two — data-defensible regulatory posture.* Score from internal audit on data + AI governance. Trending up. *Boards care about audit findings.*

*KPI three — vendor lock-in exposure.* For each top vendor, switching cost in months and dollars. Should trend down or stay flat. *Boards understand strategic risk.*

*KPI four — AI value realised vs projected.* From the business cases approved in the last 12 months, how much projected ROI materialised. *Board's truth test on the AI strategy.*

These four are CDO-credible. *Board directors will act on them.*

> [S4 ▸ slide change · t ≈ 2:30]

> [S4 ▸ reveal 1 · 4 KPIs to retire]

Four KPIs to stop reporting to the board.

*Retire — data quality score percentage.* Doesn't translate to action. Directors can't tell if 87% is good.

*Retire — catalog completeness.* Strategically right but invisible at board level. Move to team KPI.

*Retire — number of AI use cases in pipeline.* Easy to game. Doesn't measure value.

*Retire — number of trained users.* Activity metric, not outcome metric.

Stop reporting these. *Boards lose interest in CDO reports because they sound like activity, not outcomes.*
